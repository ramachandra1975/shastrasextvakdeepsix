Proliferation I: Vyākaraṇa and Nyāya

The Yogically-Formed Citta as the Instrument of Śāstric Discrimination — Pāṇini's Grammar, Bhartṛhari's Sphoṭa-Theory, and the Nyāya School's Systematic Account of Valid Inference as the Two Primary Disciplines through which Discriminative Knowledge Achieves Public, Transmissible Form

Where Part Seven Stands in the Series

Part Six closed by identifying its third limit as the gap between viveka-khyāti — the direct, non-discursive discriminative apprehension of the puruṣa-prakṛti distinction by a yogically-formed citta — and śāstric knowledge as discursive, grammatically-structured, logically-organised, and institutionally-transmissible understanding. The Yoga-śāstric account provides the deepest psychological account of what the discriminating instrument is and how it is formed; but it does not provide the account of what that instrument produces when it operates within the specifically linguistic and inferential frameworks through which discriminative knowledge becomes public property rather than private achievement. Part Seven exists because those frameworks — vyākaraṇa (grammar) and nyāya (logic and epistemology) — are themselves full śāstric systems with their own technical depth, their own foundational figures (Pāṇini and Akṣapāda Gautama, respectively), and their own ways of articulating the relationship between the forms of thought and the forms of language that this series has been developing since Part One's account of Vāk.

The specific problem that vyākaraṇa and nyāya exist to address is the problem Part Six Section 11.3 named precisely: the vikalpa-vṛtti's capacity to generate internally coherent verbal constructions that have no extra-linguistic referents — "square circles," "hare's horns," and, at the philosophically most consequential level, subtly erroneous metaphysical constructions (like the misidentification of puruṣa and citta that avidyā produces) that are linguistically well-formed and internally coherent but false. The risk of language's productive capacity is not merely academic; it is the risk that a tradition organised around the transmission of precise knowledge through linguistic means runs constantly, and that both Pāṇini's grammar and the Nyāya school's logic-and-epistemology system exist to manage with systematic technical precision.

The Handoff Points from Part Six

Three handoff points determine Part Seven's structure. First, Part Six's account of the vikalpa-vṛtti (Section III of Part Six) established the specific cognitive risk that śāstric grammar and logic exist to address: the risk of verbal constructions that generate pseudo-knowledge through linguistic productivity divorced from perceptual and inferential grounding. Second, Part Six's identification of the yogically-formed citta as the instrument through which discriminative knowledge becomes maximally reliable (Section X of Part Seven's subject-matter) establishes the relationship between discipline and śāstric competence: the practitioner who applies Pāṇini's grammatical metalanguage or Gautama's inference-theory with full command is, in the tradition's implicit psychology, a practitioner whose citta has achieved sufficient ekāgratā (one-pointedness) to hold the relevant formal structures in clear focus long enough for their discriminative application to be reliable. Third, Part Six's account of the saṃskāra-formation process (Section VIII of Part Six) establishes the mechanism through which years of grammatical or logical training deposits the relevant discriminative capacities as stable saṃskāras — cognitive habits of precision — in the citta, making the expert grammarian's or logician's discriminative responses as automatic and reliable as the trained performer's abhinaya-responses.

Abstract

This paper develops, across twelve sections, a full technical and philosophical reconstruction of vyākaraṇa and nyāya as the two primary śāstric disciplines through which the yogically-formed citta's discriminative capacity achieves public, transmissible, institutionally-reproducible form. The paper proceeds as follows. First, it establishes the concept of "śāstric proliferation" — the process by which the tradition's foundational insight into the relationship between sound, meaning, and cognition (developed across Parts I–III as the Vāk-to-Śabda progression) generates, through systematic technical elaboration in specialised domains, the family of śāstric disciplines that constitute classical Indian intellectual culture's characteristic achievement. Second, it reconstructs Pāṇini's Aṣṭādhyāyī's architecture — 3,959 sūtras in fourteen adhyāyas, organised through the Śiva-sūtras' fourteen pratyāhāra-defining phonemic groups — as the systematic grammar of Sanskrit that constitutes both the world's first generative grammar and the tradition's most ambitious attempt to capture, in a finite set of rules, the complete generative capacity of natural language. Third, it develops the Aṣṭādhyāyī's metalanguage — the technical device-vocabulary of it, anuvṛtti, adhikāra, paribhāṣā, and others — as a system of compressed rule-formulation whose economy of means is the grammatical system's analogue of the Yogasūtra's sūtra-compression, and whose operation requires a trained citta capable of holding the entire rule-system's relevant portions in active working memory simultaneously. Fourth, it examines the sphoṭa-theory — the doctrine, developed from hints in Pāṇini through Bhartṛhari's Vākyapadīya to the major Mīmāṃsā and Nyāya debates about śabda's permanent or impermanent nature — as the tradition's most sustained account of what a word is in itself: not the physical sound-sequence that is its vehicle but the unitary, indivisible meaning-bearing unit that the sound-sequence reveals, whose philosophical implications extend directly into the series' account of Vāk's levels. Fifth, it examines Bhartṛhari's Vākyapadīya in detail — particularly the pratibhā-concept (the flash of integrated understanding, the sentence-meaning's sudden, holistic apprehension) — as the philosophical culmination of the grammatical tradition's engagement with the relationship between linguistic structure and the moment of understanding. Sixth, it reconstructs the Nyāya-Sūtra of Akṣapāda Gautama as the foundational text of the nyāya (analytic reasoning) tradition, developing its sixteen padārthas (categories of philosophical analysis) as a complete taxonomy of the elements required for rigorous philosophical debate and valid knowledge-production. Seventh, it develops the five-membered nyāya inference (pratijñā, hetu, udāharaṇa, upanaya, nigamana) as both a formal logical structure and a pedagogical tool for making the move from particular observation to general conclusion reliable and publicly verifiable. Eighth, it examines the pramāṇa-theory the Nyāya system develops — four sources of valid knowledge (pratyakṣa, anumāna, upamāna, śabda) — in relationship to the Yoga-śāstric account of the vṛtti-types and the conditions under which each pramāṇa-type operates reliably or unreliably. Ninth, it addresses vyākaraṇa and nyāya as institutional frameworks — systems of training, examination, debate, and transmission — through which the discriminative capacities they develop become socially distributed rather than individually-held achievements. Tenth, it develops the series' central argument for Part Seven: that the yogically-formed citta and the śāstrically-trained citta are complementary rather than competing accounts of what a well-formed discriminative instrument is, and that the tradition's implicit ideal is their conjunction in a practitioner whose yogic depth of attentional formation is matched by śāstric precision of discriminative knowledge. Eleventh, it specifies three limits preparing Part Eight's engagement with Arthaśāstra and Āyurveda. Twelfth, it executes the handoff.

Reading Note — This paper develops vyākaraṇa and nyāya from their own technical foundations. Part Two's account of Pāṇini and śabda-bheda is presupposed as an introduction, but Part Seven's treatment is substantially more technically detailed than Part Two's. The Yoga-śāstric framework developed in Part Six — particularly the vikalpa-vṛtti, the citta-formation account, and the saṃskāra-model — is presupposed throughout and is the bridge through which Parts VI and VII are structurally connected.

I.

The Proliferation of Śāstra: From Foundational Insight to Specialised Discipline

1.1 What "Proliferation" Names

The term "proliferation" in this paper's title names a specific intellectual-historical process: the process by which a foundational insight — in this series' account, the Vedic insight into the relationship between sound, meaning, cognition, and reality that Part One reconstructed through the Vāk-concept — generates, through the sustained, technically-rigorous elaboration of its implications in specific domains, a family of distinct śāstric disciplines that are simultaneously autonomous (each has its own technical vocabulary, its own founding texts, its own lineage of trained specialists) and genealogically related (each is a systematic working-out of implications already present in the foundational insight, made possible by the prior elaborations of adjacent disciplines). The metaphor of proliferation is deliberately biological: the śāstric disciplines proliferate from a shared root in the way a plant's branches proliferate from its root-system — each branch reaching toward a different domain of available light, each developing its own form and structure, but each drawing nourishment from the same root and contributing its own products back to the shared organism of the tradition.

The particular proliferation-pattern this paper examines — Vedic phonology and hermeneutics generating Pāṇinian grammar, which generates Bhartṛhari's philosophy of language, which exists in productive tension with the Mīmāṃsā tradition's śabda-account, which in turn debates with the Nyāya tradition's pramāṇa-theory — is not a historical accident but a structural necessity: each discipline's elaboration of a domain of the foundational insight necessarily raises questions it cannot answer from within its own technical resources, and those unanswered questions motivate the adjacent discipline's development. Part Two's account of Pāṇini established the point at which phonological analysis required a grammatical system to complete it; the present paper establishes the points at which grammatical analysis requires a philosophy of language (sphoṭa-theory) and a formal logic of valid inference (nyāya) to address the problems grammar's own success generates.

1.2 The Vedāṅgas as the First Proliferation

The śāstric proliferation's first documented stage is the six Vedāṅgas — the "limbs of the Veda" — whose development, according to the tradition's own historiography, was motivated by the practical necessity of preserving the Vedic texts' phonemic integrity and hermeneutic accessibility across generations of oral transmission. The six are: śikṣā (phonetics and pronunciation), chandas (metre), vyākaraṇa (grammar), nirukta (etymology and lexical interpretation), jyotiṣa (astronomical calculation for ritual timing), and kalpa (ritual procedure). Each addresses a specific domain of technical knowledge required for correct Vedic performance and interpretation; each constitutes an autonomous discipline with its own foundational texts; and together they constitute the institutional framework within which the Vedic tradition's self-perpetuation was managed as a technical-educational enterprise rather than a merely custodial one. Part Seven's focus on vyākaraṇa and its philosophical extension into nyāya represents a choice to examine two of the Vedāṅgas' most technically elaborate and philosophically consequential developments — the choice motivated by their direct relevance to the series' central concern with the forms through which discriminative knowledge achieves public, transmissible form.

II.

Pāṇini's Aṣṭādhyāyī: The Grammar of Complete Discrimination

2.1 The Text's Achievement

Pāṇini's Aṣṭādhyāyī — the "eight-chapter [work]," composed around the fourth century BCE — constitutes one of the greatest intellectual achievements of any civilisation: a complete, generative description of Sanskrit in 3,959 sūtras, organised with a systematic economy of means so extreme that the text, like the Yogasūtra's 196 sūtras, cannot be understood without the tradition of oral commentary and trained specialist interpretation that its composition assumed as its context. Part Two's treatment of the Aṣṭādhyāyī established its foundational significance and its relationship to the Śiva-sūtras; the present section develops its technical architecture at a depth Part Two deferred.

The Aṣṭādhyāyī's principal formal achievement is its status as the world's first generative grammar in the technically precise sense: a finite system of rules capable of generating all and only the well-formed expressions of the language it describes, with no appeal to lists of particular cases beyond what the rule-system cannot capture more economically. Every sūtra is a rule; every rule is formulated in the minimal phonemic content required to carry its formal information; the sūtras are ordered within and across the eight adhyāyas in a sequence specifically designed to maximise the formal work each rule can do through the anuvṛtti (carryover) mechanism, whereby a rule's elements remain operative in subsequent rules until explicitly cancelled — so that the system's economy of expression is not the economy of a list of abbreviations but the economy of a formal system in which each element's contribution is multiplied by the number of subsequent rules it remains active in.

2.2 The Śiva-Sūtras and the Pratyāhāra System

The fourteen Śiva-sūtras — the fourteen groups of phonemes (a, i, u; ṛ, ḷ; e, o; ai, au; ha, ya, va, ra; la; ña, ma, ṅa, ṇa, na; jha, bha; gha, ḍha, dha; ja, ba, ga, ḍa, da; kha, pha, cha, ṭha, tha, ca, ṭa, ta; ka, pa; śa, ṣa, sa; ha, la) whose arrangement allows any naturally occurring class of Sanskrit phonemes to be named by a pratyāhāra — a syllable formed from the first member of a group and a marker (it) taken from the end of any subsequent group — provide the Aṣṭādhyāyī's most fundamental formal device. The pratyāhāra system transforms the Śiva-sūtras' linear arrangement into a complete addressing system for phonemic classes, allowing Pāṇini to refer to any phonologically natural class of sounds by a two-syllable name rather than by listing its members. This device is not merely economical in the sense of saving space; it is theoretically significant in the sense that Part Two established: the classes the pratyāhāras name are precisely the phonologically natural classes — the classes that recur as the domains of Sanskrit's phonological rules — whose naturalness is itself a fact about Sanskrit's phonological system rather than about Pāṇini's classificatory preferences.

2.3 The Aṣṭādhyāyī's Metalanguage: How Rules Talk About Rules

The Aṣṭādhyāyī's most technically demanding feature — and the feature most directly relevant for Part Seven's argument about the citta-requirements of śāstric discrimination — is its metalanguage: the system of technical devices through which the rules talk about themselves and about each other in ways that enable the rule-system's economy. The primary metalinguistic devices are: anuvṛtti (carryover), whereby a rule's constituent elements remain operative in subsequent rules in the same or adjacent adhyāyās until cancelled; adhikāra (heading or governing domain), whereby certain sūtras function as section-headings whose content is understood to govern all subsequent sūtras in their domain; and paribhāṣā (interpretive maxims), meta-rules that govern the application of object-level rules in cases of conflict or ambiguity. The paribhāṣās are particularly significant: they are not rules about Sanskrit but rules about rules about Sanskrit, specifying how the rule-system itself is to be interpreted and applied. Their existence demonstrates that the Aṣṭādhyāyī is not merely a descriptive inventory of Sanskrit's grammatical facts but a formal system whose self-description — whose account of how it is to be used — is itself a technically rigorous component of the system's design.

The citta-requirements of competent Aṣṭādhyāyī-application are substantial. A practitioner applying a specific rule to a specific linguistic form must simultaneously: identify the form's constituent phonemes and their phonemic class-memberships (which requires the Śiva-sūtras' pratyāhāra-system to be active in memory); identify which sūtra governs the relevant operation; determine which anuvṛtti-elements from preceding sūtras remain active in the current sūtra's domain; check for any adhikāra that governs the current rule's scope; and apply any relevant paribhāṣās to handle conflict or ambiguity between the current rule and any of its competitors in the rule-system's other sections. This is a complex, multi-layered, simultaneously-active cognitive operation that requires precisely the kind of concentrated, multi-element working-memory management that Part Six's account of dhāraṇā described as the inner triad's first achievement — the difference being that the object of the grammarian's dhāraṇā is not a single perceptual or imaginative object but the formal structure of a rule-system, and the dhyāna it approaches is not the unbroken absorption in a single object but the unbroken, simultaneous availability of the entire relevant rule-system's structure to the expert practitioner's active cognitive attention.

III.

The Metalanguage of Grammar: Devices, Economy, and the Trained Citta

3.1 It-Markers and the Scope of Technical Terms

The Aṣṭādhyāyī employs a specific class of technical markers — its (singular: it), which are phonemic segments attached to roots, suffixes, or grammatical elements within the rule-system to signal technical functions rather than to represent any phoneme of the object-language Sanskrit itself. An it-marker may signal: that a suffix triggers a specific additional operation (like the lengthening of the preceding vowel, or the addition of a specific augment); that a root or stem belongs to a specific conjugation-class; or that a specific morphological junction-environment triggers a specific phonological rule. The its are "erased" in the actual derived form — they are instructions to the rule-system, not sounds in the output — and their correct identification in a given sūtra is a prerequisite for the sūtra's correct application.

The it-marker system is, structurally, a metalinguistic level: a set of signals embedded within the language of the rules that govern how those rules are to be processed, rather than signals about the object-language Sanskrit itself. Part Two's account of śabda-bheda established that the Indian grammatical tradition was from its earliest stages sensitive to the distinction between the primary linguistic level (the language described) and the metalinguistic level (the language used to describe it); the Aṣṭādhyāyī's it-marker system is the fullest and most technically sophisticated realisation of that metalinguistic awareness in the tradition's grammatical literature.

3.2 Blocking and the Principle of Specificity

One of the Aṣṭādhyāyī's most theoretically significant organisational principles — and one of its primary contributions to formal linguistics' subsequent history — is the principle of specificity (nitya/anitya, utsarga/apavāda — general/specific, rule/exception): when two rules that could both apply in a given environment are in apparent conflict, the more specific rule (apavāda) blocks the more general one (utsarga), and the more specific rule's output is the correct derivation. This principle, along with the related paribhāṣā "the specific (apavāda) supersedes the general (utsarga)," constitutes the Aṣṭādhyāyī's primary mechanism for handling the inevitable conflicts that arise in any finite rule-system applied to the full complexity of a natural language's morphological and phonological behaviour. Its philosophical significance extends beyond formal linguistics: the principle that specific knowledge supersedes general knowledge in cases of conflict — that a more precisely characterised instance of a category takes precedence over the category's general characterisation — is a principle of discriminative reasoning whose application extends across all the śāstric disciplines Part Seven is examining, and whose deepest philosophical expression is precisely the viveka-khyāti that Part Six identified as the Yoga-śāstric practice's culminating cognitive achievement: the most specific possible discrimination (puruṣa from prakṛti at the level of their ultimate nature) blocking all less specific discriminations that would confuse them.

IV.

Sphoṭa: The Word-Meaning Unit and the Philosophy of Linguistic Reference

4.1 The Problem Sphoṭa-Theory Addresses

The sphoṭa-theory — most fully developed by Bhartṛhari in the Vākyapadīya (fifth century CE), though anticipations are found in Pāṇini's own notation and in the Mahābhāṣya of Patañjali the grammarian (distinct from Patañjali the Yoga-śāstra author) — addresses a specific and genuinely difficult problem about linguistic reference: what exactly is it that carries meaning in language? The obvious candidate answer — the particular, physically-realised sound-sequence that a speaker produces on a given occasion — immediately faces a set of problems. Two different speakers' utterances of the same word are physically distinct sound-sequences (different fundamental frequencies, different durations, different timbres), yet they carry the same meaning. The same speaker's two successive utterances of the same word are also physically distinct sound-sequences. The physically-produced sound-sequence is, moreover, a temporal entity whose successive phonemes are produced and vanish in sequence; no two phonemes of a polysyllabic word are simultaneously present in the physical sound-stream, yet the word's meaning is grasped as a unity, not as a sequence of discrete meanings. What, then, is the stable, identical, meaning-bearing entity that different physical utterances of the same word all reveal or instantiate?

Sphoṭa is the tradition's answer: the word, in itself, is not the physical sound-sequence (dhvani) but the unitary, indivisible, meaning-bearing entity that the physical sound-sequence reveals (sphūṭayati — "makes manifest"). Sphoṭa is eternal (nitya) and unchanging — different physical utterances of the same word do not produce different sphoṭas but manifest the same sphoṭa through different physical vehicles. The sphoṭa is what a hearer grasps when they understand a word, regardless of the specific physical characteristics of the utterance that occasions that grasp. And it is the sphoṭa — not the dhvani — whose relationship to artha (meaning) constitutes the linguistic sign's proper account, because it is the sphoṭa that is stable, identical across utterances, and directly connected to the meaning its physical vehicle reveals.

4.2 Sphoṭa and the Vāk-Levels of Part One

The sphoṭa-theory's philosophical significance for this series is most clearly visible when it is set in relationship to Part One's account of Vāk's four levels (parā, paśyantī, madhyamā, vaikharī). The vaikharī level — the level of physically-produced, heard speech — is precisely the dhvani-level at which the physical sound-sequence is produced and received: the level of manifested, differentiated, temporally-extended speech. The sphoṭa exists, in Bhartṛhari's account, at the madhyamā level — the level of internally-organised, pre-vocalised, structurally complete speech-intention that a speaker holds prior to its vaikharī externalisation, and that a hearer grasps in the act of understanding beyond the vaikharī sound-stream through which that understanding is occasioned. The connection establishes that the sphoṭa-theory is not an isolated grammatical-philosophical speculation but the grammatical tradition's articulation of a principle already present in the Vedic Vāk-analysis: the principle that the fully meaning-bearing level of language is not the level of physical sound but the level of structured, intentional, meaning-organised speech that the physical sound vehicle reveals to a competent hearer.

V.

Bhartṛhari and the Vākyapadīya: Sentence, Flash, and the Limit of Grammatical Analysis

5.1 The Sentence as the Primary Linguistic Unit

Bhartṛhari's Vākyapadīya — the "treatise on sentences and words" — is the philosophical culmination of the grammatical tradition's engagement with the sphoṭa-problem and its implications. Its central philosophical claim, argued across three kāṇḍas (books) of sustained technical and philosophical analysis, is that the sentence (vākya), not the word (pada), is the primary linguistic unit — the unit that is, in itself, meaning-bearing in the fullest sense — and that words are abstractions from sentences rather than the other way around. This claim directly inverts the compositional picture of language's meaning-structure that might seem most natural (sentences mean what they mean because of the meanings of their component words, combined according to syntactic rules); Bhartṛhari argues instead that words' meanings are always already understood in the context of the sentences in which they occur, and that the "meanings" of individual words abstracted from sentence-context are theoretical constructs of the grammarian's metalinguistic analysis rather than independently-grasped semantic entities.

5.2 Pratibhā: The Flash of Understanding

The concept that is Bhartṛhari's most philosophically distinctive contribution — and the concept most directly relevant for this series' account of the relationship between linguistic structure and the moment of genuine understanding — is pratibhā, sometimes translated as "linguistic intuition" or "flash of understanding" but most precisely characterised as: the holistic, instantaneous, non-sequential apprehension of a sentence's complete meaning that occurs in the hearer at the moment of genuine understanding, beyond the temporal sequence of the sentence's physical utterance and beyond the analytical sequence of the hearer's word-by-word processing. Pratibhā is the cognitive event in which the sentence's meaning arrives as a whole — a Gestalt of significance, not a sum of component word-meanings — and it is, in Bhartṛhari's account, the linguistic-cognitive event that is most directly the counterpart of the Yoga-śāstric samāpatti's moment of the object's sudden shining-forth in transparent absorption: the hearer's citta "falls into" the sentence's meaning in the same way the yogin's citta falls into its object in nirvitarka samāpatti — the verbal-conceptual overlay cleared, the meaning itself present as immediate apprehension rather than as the output of an analytical process.

वाक्यस्फोट → प्रतिभा → अर्थसाक्षात्कार
vākya-sphoṭa → pratibhā → artha-sākṣātkāra
The sentence's unitary meaning-unit (vākya-sphoṭa) occasions, in a competent hearer's citta, the flash of holistic understanding (pratibhā) that is the direct apprehension (sākṣātkāra) of the sentence's artha — parallel, in structure and in the quality of immediacy and holistic grasp it involves, to the nirvitarka-samāpatti's transparent absorption in an object's own form. The grammarian's highest technical achievement is the analysis that makes pratibhā reliable; the moment of pratibhā itself exceeds analytical control and is, like samādhi, something that analysis can prepare but cannot produce.

VI.

Nyāya-Sūtra: The Sixteen Padārthas of Philosophical Analysis

6.1 Nyāya's Foundational Concern

The Nyāya-Sūtra of Akṣapāda Gautama (approximately second century CE) — with the extensive and philosophically rich Bhāṣya of Vātsyāyana as its primary authorised commentary — establishes nyāya (literally: that which leads to a conclusion, rule of reasoning, correct procedure) as the tradition's systematic account of valid philosophical argumentation and valid knowledge-production. Where vyākaraṇa addresses the linguistic form of knowledge-statements (what makes a Sanskrit sentence well-formed), nyāya addresses their epistemic credentials (what makes a knowledge-claim valid and what makes it invalid). The two disciplines are complementary at the most fundamental level: a claim must be linguistically well-formed to be assessable at all (vyākaraṇa's domain) and must be epistemically grounded to count as knowledge rather than mere assertion (nyāya's domain); the tradition that trains practitioners in both is equipping them with the full discriminative apparatus that valid knowledge-production requires.

1 प्रमाण Pramāṇa

Valid sources of knowledge: pratyakṣa (perception), anumāna (inference), upamāna (comparison/analogy), śabda (testimony). The four instruments through which valid knowledge is legitimately obtained.

2 प्रमेय Prameya

Objects of valid knowledge: self, body, senses, sense-objects, cognition, mind, activity, defects, rebirth, fruit of action, suffering, and liberation. The complete domain of what can be known.

3 संशय Saṃśaya

Doubt: the cognitive state in which multiple, competing cognitions of the same object are simultaneously present without resolution. The condition that motivates inquiry and whose resolution constitutes knowledge's achievement.

4 प्रयोजन Prayojana

Purpose or motive: the aim toward which inquiry is directed, whose specification is required for inquiry to be properly organised and its conclusion evaluable as success or failure.

5 दृष्टान्त Dṛṣṭānta

Illustrative example: the particular case (the kitchen, the fire, the smoke) that grounds the inference's general claim in observed fact and makes the inference's logical structure publicly verifiable.

6 सिद्धान्त Siddhānta

Established doctrine: claims already accepted as valid by the relevant śāstric tradition, serving as the agreed background against which new claims are assessed. The institutional memory of established knowledge.

7 अवयव Avayava

The members or limbs of the syllogism: the five-membered structure (pratijñā, hetu, udāharaṇa, upanaya, nigamana) through which a valid inference is articulated in its complete, publicly-checkable form.

8 तर्क Tarka

Hypothetical reasoning: the reductio-type argument that establishes a claim's validity by demonstrating the absurdity of its denial. A secondary instrument of valid reasoning, not itself a pramāṇa but a support for inference.

9 निर्णय Nirṇaya

Decisive conclusion: the resolution of doubt through the application of the pramāṇas to the inquiry's object, resulting in a stable cognitive state of established knowledge rather than the oscillating state of saṃśaya.

10 वाद Vāda

Sincere philosophical debate: the form of argumentative exchange in which both parties are genuinely interested in reaching the correct conclusion and accept the pramāṇas and avayavas as binding constraints. Distinguished from jalpa and vitaṇḍā.

11 जल्प Jalpa

Disputatious debate: argumentative exchange in which a party is motivated by the desire to win rather than by the desire to reach truth, permitting the use of sophistical techniques (chala, jāti, nigrahasthāna) not available in vāda.

12 वितण्डा Vitaṇḍā

Destructive debate: the form in which a party's aim is purely to refute the opponent's position without advancing any position of one's own. Logically parasitic, but recognised as a real phenomenon in the tradition of philosophical exchange.

13 हेत्वाभास Hetvābhāsa

Fallacious reasons: the five varieties of defective hetu (erratic, contradictory, equal-and-opposite, unproven, mistimed) that produce the appearance of valid inference without its reality. The taxonomy of formal fallacies.

14 छल Chala

Sophistical objection: the illegitimate technique of intentionally misinterpreting an opponent's words (by taking a term in a non-intended sense) to produce the appearance of refutation without genuinely engaging the opponent's argument.

15 जाति Jāti

Futile objection: the class of responses to an argument that have the surface form of refutations but that, on analysis, depend on illegitimate analogical extensions or on ignoring the argument's specific logical structure.

16 निग्रहस्थान Nigrahasthāna

Points of defeat: the conditions under which a party in philosophical debate has lost the exchange — misunderstanding the opponent's position, failing to respond to a valid argument, self-contradiction, abandoning one's own thesis. The institutional mechanism for enforcing the rules of valid exchange.

VII.

Anumāna: The Five-Membered Inference and the Logic of Valid Conclusion

7.1 The Five Members and Their Roles

The nyāya inference's five-membered (pañcāvayava) structure is the tradition's most distinctive formal contribution to the history of logic — distinctive not because five-membered inference is a different logical relationship from the two-premise syllogism of Western Aristotelian logic (the logical relationship is structurally the same) but because the five-membered form includes both the formal components that carry the inference's logical validity and the epistemic-pedagogical components that make the inference's validity publicly verifiable to an audience that was not party to the original observation. The five members are:

प्रतिज्ञा Pratijñā
The thesis: the statement of what is to be proved. "There is fire on the hill." The pratijñā names both the subject (the hill, pakṣa) and the property to be established (fire, sādhya). It is a claim rather than an observation; its status as a claim — something requiring proof — is what motivates the remaining members.
↓
हेतु Hetu
The reason: the observed property that grounds the inference. "Because there is smoke." The hetu must be present in the pakṣa (the hill really does have smoke), must be found in all cases where the sādhya (fire) is found (vyāpti — universal concomitance), and must be absent in all cases where the sādhya is absent. These three conditions constitute the hetu's formal requirements for validity.
↓
उदाहरण Udāharaṇa
The illustrative example: both a positive instance (sapakṣa — "wherever there is smoke there is fire, as in the kitchen") and optionally a negative instance (vipakṣa — "wherever there is no fire there is no smoke, as in a lake"). The udāharaṇa makes the vyāpti (universal concomitance between hetu and sādhya) publicly observable rather than merely asserted, grounding the inference's general claim in perceptible particular cases.
↓
उपनय Upanaya
The application: the explicit subsumption of the pakṣa (the hill) under the general rule established by the udāharaṇa. "The hill is such a case" — connecting the general rule ("smoke implies fire") to the particular case ("this hill has smoke") in an explicit step that could not be omitted without producing a logical gap in the inference's public structure.
↓
निगमन Nigamana
The conclusion: the restatement of the thesis (pratijñā), now established by the inference's prior members. "Therefore there is fire on the hill." The nigamana's explicit restatement of what the pratijñā asserted closes the inference's logical arc, marking the transition from the pratijñā's status as a claim to its status as an established conclusion — the nirṇaya (decisive resolution of doubt) that the Nyāya system identifies as the goal of valid philosophical inquiry.

7.2 Vyāpti: The Universal Concomitance and Its Epistemic Problems

The vyāpti — the universal concomitance between hetu (smoke) and sādhya (fire) that the inference depends on — is the nyāya inference's philosophically most demanding element and the locus of the tradition's most sustained internal debate. The problem is classic: a universal claim (wherever smoke, there fire — always, without exception) cannot be established by any finite number of observed positive instances, because the next instance might be the counterexample that falsifies the universal. The Nyāya tradition's responses to this problem — the debate between those who ground vyāpti-knowledge in a specific perceptual capacity (vyāpti-perception, the direct perceptual grasp of the invariant connection between co-present properties in observed instances) and those who ground it in the repeated accumulation of confirmatory observations combined with the absence of known counterexamples — constitute one of the classical Indian philosophical tradition's deepest and most sustained epistemological debates, and their resolution (or non-resolution) has direct implications for the question of how any śāstric generalisation — including the Nāṭyaśāstra's rasa-assignments and the Yoga-śāstra's citta-formation claims — is epistemically justified.

VIII.

Pramāṇa-Theory: The Four Sources of Valid Knowledge

8.1 Pratyakṣa: Perception as the Foundational Pramāṇa

The Nyāya-Sūtra's definition of pratyakṣa (perception) specifies it as: indriyārthsannikarṣotpannaṃ jñānam avyapadeśyam avyabhicāri vyavasāyātmakam — "knowledge produced by the contact of a sense-organ with its object, non-linguistic, non-errant, and determinate." Four elements of this definition carry theoretical weight for the series' argument. "Contact of a sense-organ with its object" establishes perception's causal basis in the physical relationship between the sense-faculty and its object-domain — a relationship that the Yoga-śāstric account would locate within the citta's engagement with gross sense-objects through the manas-faculty's coordination of sensory input. "Non-linguistic" (avyapadeśya) specifies that genuine perceptual knowledge is not constituted by its verbal formulation — the perception is distinct from and prior to any linguistic expression of it, establishing a significant distance between the Nyāya account of perception and the philosophical positions (associated with Bhartṛhari's school) that regard all cognition as linguistically-constituted. "Non-errant" (avyabhicāri) specifies that the perception must not be subject to the standard perceptual errors (the rope-snake illusion, the shell-silver illusion) that produce false perceptual appearances. And "determinate" (vyavasāyātmaka) specifies that perceptual knowledge involves a definite judgment — "this is a pot," not merely an undifferentiated sensory input — that is the product of the citta's integration of sensory data rather than of its raw reception.

8.2 Anumāna, Upamāna, Śabda: The Three Non-Perceptual Pramāṇas

The three non-perceptual pramāṇas — anumāna (inference), upamāna (comparison or analogical knowledge), and śabda (testimony, literally "word") — extend the domain of valid knowledge-production beyond what direct sensory contact can provide. Anumāna's structure has been developed in Section VII above; its relationship to the Yoga-śāstric account of the citta's discriminative capacity is most clearly visible in the vyāpti-knowledge question: the universal concomitance that a valid inference requires is precisely the kind of stable, reliable, over-instance-general cognitive content that the yogically-formed citta's saṃskāra-structure — enriched by the accumulated discriminative training of sustained śāstric practice — is most capable of holding with confidence. Upamāna (comparison) is the knowledge produced by the recognition of a similarity between an object previously described to the perceiver and an object currently perceived — the traveller who, having been told "a gavaya is like a cow but has a larger body," recognises a gavaya in the forest through its similarity to the cow they already know. Śabda (testimony) is knowledge produced through the reliable testimony of a competent, honest speaker — including, most significantly for the śāstric tradition's self-understanding, the testimony of the Vedic texts themselves, whose authority the Nyāya and Mīmāṃsā traditions debate extensively in terms of whether Veda-testimony is a distinct pramāṇa-type or a species of śabda in general.

IX.

Vyākaraṇa and Nyāya as Institutional Frameworks for Distributed Discrimination

9.1 The Śāstric Discipline as Social Technology

The individually-achieved discriminative capacities that vyākaraṇa-training and nyāya-training produce — the grammarian's capacity for linguistically precise knowledge-formulation; the logician's capacity for epistemically rigorous knowledge-assessment — are, in both cases, the products of institutional frameworks that are as important as the technical disciplines themselves for the social distribution of discriminative knowledge that the tradition aims at. The gurukula (teacher's household) as the primary educational institution; the debate-hall (śāstrārtha) as the institutional context for publicly-performed nyāya-inference; the commentarial tradition as the institutional mechanism for accumulating and transmitting interpretive precision across generations — these are the social structures through which vyākaraṇa and nyāya achieve their function as what this paper calls distributed discrimination: the capacity for the tradition's individual members to contribute to, draw on, and check each other's discriminative work within a shared framework of epistemic standards.

9.2 The Tradition of Debate and the Public Verifiability of Knowledge

The Nyāya system's explicit attention to the conditions of valid philosophical debate — the sixteen padārthas' inclusion of vāda, jalpa, vitaṇḍā, chala, jāti, and nigrahasthāna as technical categories governing the practice of philosophical exchange — reveals an awareness that the social context of knowledge-production is itself a technical matter requiring systematic specification. A claim that is formally valid as an inference (correct hetu, correct vyāpti, correctly performed five members) but that has been derived through illegitimate debating techniques (chala-sophistry, jāti-futility) is not epistemically trustworthy in the context of public philosophical exchange, because the context's integrity — the mutual commitment of participants to the valid-knowledge-seeking mode of vāda rather than the victory-seeking mode of jalpa — is itself a precondition of the exchange's epistemic value. The Nyāya tradition's explicit theorisation of these contextual preconditions is, from the series' perspective, a recognition that the quality of the attending minds — their adherence to the pramāṇas, their freedom from the kleśas of pride and desire for victory that drive jalpa rather than vāda — is constitutive of the knowledge the exchange can produce. This is the institutional expression of Part Six's claim that the quality of the attending citta determines the quality of what is attended to: applied to the social-institutional context of philosophical exchange, it yields the claim that the quality of the debating participants' cognitive and ethical formation determines the quality of the knowledge their exchange can produce.

X.

The Yogically-Formed Citta as the Instrument of Śāstric Discrimination

10.1 The Complementarity Thesis

This paper's central argument — developed through the preceding sections' treatments of vyākaraṇa and nyāya — is the complementarity thesis: that the yogically-formed citta (Part Six's subject-matter) and the śāstrically-trained citta (Part Seven's subject-matter) are not competing accounts of what a well-formed discriminative instrument is, with each tradition claiming priority for its own mode of formation, but complementary accounts of two dimensions of the same ideal. The yogically-formed citta is one in which the capacity for sustained, concentrated, non-reactive, absorbed attention has been developed to the degree where the kleśas' distorting effect on cognition has been significantly reduced; the śāstrically-trained citta is one in which the specific technical frameworks (grammatical precision, inferential validity-checking, pramāṇa-application) for operating on linguistically-formulated knowledge-claims with reliability have been installed as stable cognitive saṃskāras. The tradition's implicit ideal — the ideal toward which the gurukula system's combined training in yoga (through the yama-niyama limbs as the educational context's ethical foundation) and śāstra (through the grammatical and logical disciplines as the curriculum's intellectual content) aimed — is their conjunction: a citta both yogically stable enough for its discriminative work to be uncorrupted by kleśa-driven distortion and śāstrically equipped enough for that discrimination to operate within the technical frameworks that make it publicly reliable and institutionally transmissible.

10.2 The Series' Developing Argument: Vāk's Proliferation and Return

Part Seven marks the series' turning-point in a specific structural sense. Parts I through VI traced a progressive deepening from the most explicit, vaikharī-level linguistic phenomena (the manifested sounds of Vedic speech in Part I, the phonemic discriminations of Pāṇinian grammar in Part II) toward the most interior, paśyantī-level processes (the yogin's inner attentional discipline of Part VI, approaching the parā-level's ground of undivided awareness). Parts VII through XII trace the return movement: the same progressive deepening now applied to the tradition's concrete social and intellectual history (the śāstric disciplines of Part VII, the applied śāstras of Part VIII, the mantra-technology of Part IX, the historical case studies of Part X, the ethical-metaphysical synthesis of Part XI) — a return movement in which the deep interior developed in Parts I–VI manifests as the productive capacity of a tradition that can proliferate into increasingly diverse and technically sophisticated disciplines precisely because its roots are sunk deep enough. Vyākaraṇa and nyāya are the first two instances of this proliferating return: the yogically-stable, phonemically-precise, affectively-formed, aesthetically-educated citta (Parts I–VI's product) now expressing itself outward as the grammatical precision and logical rigour through which the tradition manages its own knowledge-production at the institutional level.

XI.

Limits and the Forward Problem

11.1 The Limit of Linguistic and Logical Form: The Body's Return

The discriminative capacities that vyākaraṇa and nyāya develop operate, both of them, in the domain of linguistically-formulated knowledge: knowledge that can be stated, checked, debated, and transmitted in the form of Sanskrit sentences whose grammatical precision can be evaluated against the Aṣṭādhyāyī's rules and whose epistemic credentials can be assessed against the Nyāya-Sūtra's pramāṇa-theory and avayava-structure. But not all knowledge of the kind this series has been examining is or can be linguistically-formulated in this way. The knowledge that a classical Bharatanāṭyam dancer has of how to execute a specific karaṇa — the knowledge that Part Five's Section 3.4's Chidambaram analysis established as residing in the sculptural programme precisely because it cannot be fully specified in verbal form — is an embodied, procedural, skill-level knowledge that the grammatical and logical frameworks of vyākaraṇa and nyāya cannot in principle capture. Part Eight's treatment of Āyurveda will address precisely this knowledge-type — the knowledge of the body's own organisation and the principles governing its health, disease, and restoration — and its introduction of the prāṇic and somatic frameworks that the yoga-śāstra, the abhinaya-śāstra, and the grammatical-logical śāstras all presuppose but do not themselves develop.

11.2 The Limit of Institutional Form: The Political and the Economic

Vyākaraṇa and nyāya, as institutional frameworks for distributed discrimination, operate within a social context that neither discipline theorises from within its own technical resources: the context of political organisation, economic structure, and the management of social conflict that makes the gurukula and the debate-hall possible as stable institutional forms. Part Eight's treatment of the Arthaśāstra — the most rigorous Indian account of political economy and statecraft — addresses this context directly, examining the social-institutional substrate within which the śāstric disciplines operate and the account of human motivation and social organisation that makes the tradition's educational and intellectual institutions possible as historical achievements.

11.3 The Limit of the Ordinary: The Mantra's Transgressive Claim

The third limit is the most fundamental for the series' overall trajectory: vyākaraṇa and nyāya treat language as an instrument of valid knowledge-production within the domain of ordinary experience and ordinary cognitive operations. Both assume that the knowledge-seeker is operating within the ordinary citta's vṛtti-activities — however well-disciplined by yogic training — and that the goal is the reliable production of valid knowledge-claims expressible in well-formed Sanskrit propositions. But Part Nine's subject-matter — mantra-śāstra — makes a claim that exceeds both of these assumptions: that specific sound-sequences, recited or intoned in specific conditions of citta-concentration and ritual context, produce effects that cannot be accounted for within the ordinary pramāṇa-framework of perception, inference, analogy, and testimony — that the mantra is, in the fullest sense, a technology of Vāk's parā-level, operating not through linguistic reference to extra-linguistic reality but through direct identity with the primal vibration from which both language and reality emerge. This claim is Part Nine's starting-point, and it represents the series' return to Part One's deepest theme — the non-dual ground from which Vāk's differentiation into phoneme, metre, grammar, logic, and all the rest proceeds — through the specifically technical and ritual route of the mantra-tradition's own account of what sound ultimately is and what it can ultimately do.

XII.

Forward to Part Eight: Applied Śāstra — Arthaśāstra and Āyurveda

12.1 What This Paper Has Established

This paper has developed ten principal results. First, it established the concept of śāstric proliferation as the structural process through which the tradition's foundational Vāk-insight generates the family of specialised śāstric disciplines. Second, it reconstructed the Aṣṭādhyāyī's architecture — its sūtra-economy, its Śiva-sūtras' pratyāhāra-foundation, its metalanguage of anuvṛtti, adhikāra, and paribhāṣā. Third, it developed the metalanguage's citta-requirements, establishing the structural parallel between grammatical rule-system competence and the concentrated multi-element working-memory management that Part Six's dhāraṇā-account described. Fourth, it developed the sphoṭa-theory as the grammatical tradition's account of what a word is in itself, connecting it to Part One's Vāk-levels at the madhyamā-vaikharī interface. Fifth, it examined Bhartṛhari's Vākyapadīya, particularly the pratibhā-concept as the linguistic-cognitive counterpart of the nirvitarka-samāpatti's transparent object-apprehension. Sixth, it reconstructed the Nyāya-Sūtra's sixteen padārthas as a complete taxonomy of philosophical analysis. Seventh, it developed the five-membered inference's structure and the vyāpti-problem's epistemological depth. Eighth, it examined the four pramāṇas and their relationship to the Yoga-śāstric citta-formation account. Ninth, it addressed vyākaraṇa and nyāya as institutional frameworks for distributed discrimination. Tenth, it developed the complementarity thesis — the yogically-formed and śāstrically-trained citta as complementary dimensions of the tradition's implicit ideal of well-formed discriminative instruments — as the series' central argument for Part Seven.

Preview of Part Eight: Proliferation II — Arthaśāstra and Āyurveda

Part Eight will examine the Arthaśāstra of Kauṭilya and the Carakasaṃhitā and Suśrutasaṃhitā of the Āyurvedic tradition as the two applied śāstric disciplines in which the series' developing account of the relationship between the trained citta, the disciplined body, and the structured social environment achieves its most socially-embedded and practically-grounded expression. The Arthaśāstra's account of rājanīti (statecraft) as a śāstra — a technically specified discipline with its own foundational text, its own technical vocabulary, and its own account of valid knowledge and valid method in the political domain — will be examined as the tradition's most developed attempt to apply the kind of discriminative rigour Part Seven has examined in the linguistic and logical domains to the domain of human social and political organisation. The Āyurvedic tradition's account of the body — the doṣa-system (vāta, pitta, kapha), the dhātu-theory of bodily constitution, the concept of prāṇa as the vital principle organising the body's functions — will be examined as the somatic knowledge-framework whose absence from both the Yoga-śāstric and the grammatical-logical accounts Part Seven's Section 11.1 identified as a genuine limit.

The grammarian who knows the rule and the situation but cannot hold both simultaneously in a sufficiently concentrated and organised form of attention will mis-apply the rule. The logician who knows the inference-form but whose citta is driven by the desire to win rather than the desire to know will employ jalpa rather than vāda and will produce the appearance of knowledge rather than knowledge itself. Vyākaraṇa and nyāya are, at their deepest level, not merely technical disciplines but specifications of what it is to be the kind of knowing subject — stable, concentrated, motivated by truth rather than by victory, formed by practice toward discriminative reliability — that the śāstric tradition's knowledge-producing activities require. They presuppose the yogically-formed citta because they are the disciplinary contexts within which that citta's discriminative capacity achieves its most technically rigorous expression. And the tradition that trains both, in sequence and together, is not training two separate kinds of expertise but a single, integrated, complementary capacity for knowing well. Glossary

स्फोट sphoṭa
The unitary, indivisible, meaning-bearing word-unit revealed (sphūṭyate) by the physical sound-sequence (dhvani) without being identical to it. Bhartṛhari's Vākyapadīya's central technical concept: the entity that is stable and identical across multiple physical utterances of the same word, and whose relationship to artha (meaning) constitutes the linguistic sign's proper account.
प्रतिभा pratibhā
The flash of holistic understanding; the instantaneous, non-sequential apprehension of a sentence's complete meaning that occurs in a competent hearer at the moment of genuine understanding. Bhartṛhari's most philosophically distinctive concept: the cognitive event structurally parallel to nirvitarka-samāpatti's transparent object-apprehension, available in the linguistic domain.
अनुवृत्ति anuvṛtti
Carryover: the Aṣṭādhyāyī's device whereby a rule's constituent elements remain operative in subsequent rules in the same section until explicitly cancelled. The primary mechanism of the grammar's sūtra-economy; one of the primary demands on the grammarian's citta, which must hold the relevant carried-over elements in active working memory.

XIII.

Kātyāyana's Vārtikas and the Three-Layer Commentarial Architecture

Section Overview — This section reconstructs the trimuni-vyākaraṇa (the "grammar of the three sages") as a layered system of progressive technical correction, examines the vārtika as a distinct logical genre, traces Patañjali's Mahābhāṣya as the point at which grammar becomes explicit philosophy of language, and develops the commentarial architecture's implications for the series' account of distributed, trans-generational discrimination.

13.1 The Three Sages and the Logic of Successive Refinement

The Aṣṭādhyāyī did not stand alone as a closed and self-sufficient text. Within a few centuries of its composition it generated a three-layer commentarial architecture — sūtra (Pāṇini, c. fourth century BCE), vārtika (Kātyāyana, c. third century BCE), and bhāṣya (Patañjali the grammarian, c. second century BCE) — that the tradition calls the trimuni-vyākaraṇa, "the grammar of the three sages." The architecture's logic is cumulative rather than merely sequential: each later layer presupposes, examines, and where necessary corrects the layer beneath it, without ever discarding it. Pāṇini's sūtras remain the base text throughout; Kātyāyana's vārtikas are composed as marginal interventions keyed to specific sūtras; and Patañjali's bhāṣya is structured as a sustained commentary that takes up sūtra and vārtika together, sūtra by sūtra, and subjects both to dialectical scrutiny before arriving at a settled interpretation. No later Sanskrit grammatical tradition — not the Kāśikā, not the Siddhānta-Kaumudī, not the later prakriyā-grammars — has dispensed with this three-layer foundation; subsequent grammatical literature builds upon it as settled bedrock rather than attempting to supersede it.

13.2 Vārtika as a Distinct Genre of Technical Correction

Kātyāyana's vārtikas are not paraphrases, simplifications, or popularisations of Pāṇini's sūtras; they belong to a distinct technical genre whose characteristic move is diagnostic rather than expository. A vārtika typically opens by constructing a counter-example — a derivable form that the sūtra as stated would wrongly license (atiprasaṅga, overgeneration) or a well-formed form that it would wrongly exclude (aprāpti, undergeneration) — and then proceeds to state the minimal additional qualification required to restore the rule-system's accuracy without disturbing any of its other correct outputs. This is diagnostic labour of an exacting kind: it requires the vārtikakāra to hold the entire derivational consequence of a given sūtra in view, to run that sūtra mentally against the full space of attested and theoretically generable Sanskrit forms, and to locate the precise point at which the rule's stated scope and its intended scope diverge. The vārtika's economy of expression mirrors the sūtra's own: a single vārtika of perhaps four or five words may close a gap that would otherwise generate hundreds of incorrect derivations across the language's morphological system.

13.3 Three Worked Examples of Vārtika-Style Correction

The pattern is best seen through worked examples of the kind the tradition itself uses for pedagogical purposes. Consider a sūtra that specifies a general operation for a class of verbal roots; a vārtika may observe that one particular root within that class, owing to an idiosyncrasy of its historical formation, behaves irregularly under the general rule, and may therefore append an exception-clause restricted to that single root — an apavāda in the precise sense Section 3.2 of this paper develops. Consider next a sūtra whose stated domain is phonologically specified through a pratyāhāra (Section 2.2); a vārtika may observe that the pratyāhāra as constructed inadvertently includes one phoneme that ought, on independent grounds, to be excluded from the rule's operation, and may therefore append a phonemic exclusion-clause. Consider finally a sūtra governing a morphological junction whose anuvṛtti-inheritance (Section 2.3) from an earlier sūtra is, on careful tracing, ambiguous between two candidate antecedents; a vārtika may simply disambiguate which antecedent is intended, closing an interpretive gap that the bare sūtra-sequence leaves open. Each of these three patterns — root-specific exception, phonemic-domain refinement, anuvṛtti-disambiguation — recurs across the roughly 4,500 vārtikas the tradition preserves, and together they constitute a near-exhaustive taxonomy of the kinds of error a finite generative rule-system applied to a natural language's full complexity is liable to generate.

13.4 Patañjali's Mahābhāṣya: The Bhāṣya as Philosophical Treatise

Patañjali's Mahābhāṣya elevates the commentarial layer a further degree: it does not merely adjudicate between Pāṇini's sūtras and Kātyāyana's vārtikas but repeatedly uses individual sūtras as occasions for sustained philosophical digression — on the nature of word-meaning, on whether the word denotes the individual (vyakti), the universal (jāti), or the configuration (ākṛti), on the ontological status of substance and quality, and on the proper relationship between śabda and artha. The Mahābhāṣya's famous ahnika ("daily lesson") discussions, particularly the Paspaśāhnika that opens the work, address questions that are squarely philosophy of language rather than narrowly technical grammar: why study grammar at all, what kind of correctness (śabda-śuddhi) it secures, and what relationship obtains between correct usage and the attainment of dharma. These discussions anticipate, by several centuries, the technical vocabulary that Bhartṛhari's sphoṭa-theory (Section IV above) will require, and they establish the precedent — followed throughout the later grammatical tradition — that a grammar's deepest commentarial layer is permitted, indeed expected, to become explicit philosophy.

13.5 The Trimuni-Vyākaraṇa as a Model of Trans-Generational Discrimination

The three-layer structure models, at the level of textual genre, the same discriminative principle this paper's Section 3.2 identifies at the level of individual rules: the specific (the vārtika's local correction, the bhāṣya's local digression) operates within and refines the general (the sūtra's stated rule) without displacing it — a commentarial apavāda/utsarga relationship that is itself an instance of the discriminative logic the grammatical tradition teaches its students to apply. But the trimuni-vyākaraṇa also extends Section 9.1's account of distributed discrimination along a dimension that section did not fully develop: discrimination distributed not merely across contemporaries within a single institutional setting (the gurukula, the debate-hall) but across three successive generations of trained cittas, each generation's discriminative output becoming the next generation's raw material. No single citta, however exhaustively trained, produced the complete, internally consistent, error-corrected grammatical system the tradition now possesses; the system is the cumulative product of a discriminative labour distributed across historical time, in which each layer's contribution is preserved, examined, and where necessary refined by the next, but never simply discarded.

XIV.

The Mīmāṃsā Counter-Position: Śabda as Eternal, Not Sphoṭa

Section Overview — This section reconstructs the Mīmāṃsā school's rival account of linguistic meaning-bearing, particularly Kumārila Bhaṭṭa's varṇa-vāda (the doctrine of the eternal phoneme), examines the debate's principal arguments on both sides, and develops the stakes of the dispute for the series' account of sequential versus holistic cognition.

14.1 The Mīmāṃsā School's Hermeneutic Priorities

The Mīmāṃsā school's primary concern, unlike the grammatical tradition's, is not the descriptive adequacy of a generative account of Sanskrit but the correct hermeneutics of Vedic ritual injunction (vidhi): how a Vedic sentence's prescriptive force is to be determined, what relationship obtains between a ritual injunction and the result it promises, and — most relevantly for this section — what kind of entity a Vedic word or sentence must be in order to retain its prescriptive authority across the indefinitely many recitations and indefinitely many hearers through which Vedic transmission proceeds. The Mīmāṃsaka's overriding concern with the Veda's authorless, eternal status (apauruṣeyatva) shapes the school's entire approach to the sphoṭa-question: any theory of linguistic meaning-bearing that the Mīmāṃsā adopts must be compatible with the claim that the Veda's words and their prescriptive force are not contingent products of any speaker's intention but eternally fixed features of language itself.

14.2 Kumārila and the Eternality of the Phoneme

The sphoṭa-theory developed in Section IV did not go unchallenged from within the broader śāstric tradition. The Mīmāṃsā school, particularly in Kumārila Bhaṭṭa's Ślokavārtika (seventh century CE) — composed as a defence of the older Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra and Śābara-Bhāṣya against Buddhist epistemological critique — develops a rival account in which it is the individual phoneme (varṇa) itself, not a supervening sphoṭa-unit, that is eternal (nitya) and that directly bears meaning in fixed combination with other eternal phonemes. On this varṇa-vāda account, what differs between two utterances of the same word is not which sphoṭa is manifested on each occasion but merely the transient sound-quality (dhvani) through which the same sequence of eternal phonemes is perceptually registered; the phonemes themselves, and their fixed combinatorial meaning-bearing capacity, never come into or pass out of existence. A word's meaning, on this account, is not revealed by its phonemes the way the sphoṭa-theorist holds it to be revealed; it is constituted directly by the phonemes themselves, in their proper sequence, without need for any additional unitary entity standing over and above them.

14.3 The Argument from Memory-Impression (Saṃskāra) and Sequential Apprehension

Kumārila's most technically developed argument against the sphoṭa-theorist turns on the mechanics of how a hearer grasps a polysyllabic word's meaning despite the physical impossibility of any two of its phonemes being simultaneously present in the air. The Mīmāṃsaka's solution does not require a unitary sphoṭa: each phoneme, as it is heard, leaves a saṃskāra (a residual mental impression) in the hearer's awareness; by the time the word's final phoneme is heard, the saṃskāras of all the preceding phonemes are still active, and it is the simultaneous co-presence of these residual impressions — not the simultaneous co-presence of the phonemes themselves, and not any additional sphoṭa-entity — that allows the hearer's citta to grasp the word's meaning as a unity. This account is explicitly compositional and additive: meaning accumulates phoneme by phoneme through the citta's retentive capacity, rather than being revealed all at once by an indivisible sphoṭa that the phonemic sequence merely occasions.

14.4 The Grammarian's Reply and the Persistence of the Dispute

Bhartṛhari and his successors do not regard the saṃskāra-account as a sufficient resolution of the sphoṭa-problem, and the Vākyapadīya develops several counter-arguments. The most pressing is that a mere accumulation of residual impressions of individually meaningless phonemes cannot, on its own, explain why the resulting cognition has the specific, determinate, holistic character of grasping one particular meaning rather than merely possessing a confused residue of several discrete phonemic traces; something must explain why the accumulated saṃskāras resolve into a single coherent cognitive content rather than remaining an unintegrated heap, and the sphoṭa-theorist holds that this resolving function is precisely what the sphoṭa supplies. The dispute between the two schools was never definitively settled within the classical tradition itself; later Mīmāṃsā authors (notably within the Prābhākara sub-school) develop refinements of the saṃskāra-account to meet the grammarian's objections, while later grammarians develop refinements of the sphoṭa-account to meet the Mīmāṃsaka's objections, and the debate continues, in modified form, into the Navya-Nyāya period discussed in Section XV below.

14.5 The Stakes of the Debate for the Series' Argument

The varṇa-vāda/sphoṭa-vāda debate is not a merely technical dispute about linguistic ontology; it is a debate about where, in the chain from physical sound to grasped meaning, stability and identity reside — and therefore about what kind of cognitive event "understanding a word" actually is. If the Mīmāṃsaka is correct, understanding is fundamentally compositional and sequential: the citta registers a temporally ordered series of eternal phonemes, retains their residual impressions, and constructs meaning additively from that retained sequence. If Bhartṛhari is correct, understanding is fundamentally holistic and instantaneous: the citta apprehends a unitary sphoṭa that the sequential phonemes merely occasion, without the meaning itself being built up piece by piece. This is the linguistic-philosophical version of the same question Part Six's account of vṛtti-sequencing raises about the citta's own temporal structure — whether mental content is irreducibly sequential or admits of genuinely non-sequential, gestalt apprehension — and the pratibhā-concept of Section 5.2 is Bhartṛhari's wager that the second alternative is correct, at least for sentence-meaning, while Kumārila's saṃskāra-account is the considered wager of an equally rigorous tradition that the first alternative suffices.

XV.

Navya-Nyāya: Gaṅgeśa and the Formalisation of Cognition

Section Overview — This section reconstructs Gaṅgeśa Upādhyāya's Tattvacintāmaṇi and the Navya-Nyāya school's technical apparatus for the precise analysis of cognition, develops worked examples of the avacchedaka/viśeṣaṇa relational vocabulary, and connects the school's hyper-formalisation to this paper's account of the citta-requirements of śāstric discrimination.

15.1 The Tattvacintāmaṇi and the Turn to Technical Precision-Language

The classical Nyāya-Sūtra's sixteen padārthas (Section VI) received, roughly a millennium after Gautama, a second great technical transformation at the hands of Gaṅgeśa Upādhyāya (thirteenth century CE), whose Tattvacintāmaṇi inaugurates the Navya-Nyāya ("New Nyāya") school. Navya-Nyāya's distinctive achievement is the development of an extraordinarily precise technical apparatus — built primarily on the abstract nouns formed with the suffixes -tā and -tva ("-ness"), and on the relational vocabulary of avacchedaka (delimitor), viśeṣaṇa (qualifier), and viśeṣya/anuyogin (qualificandum) — for specifying the exact logical structure of a cognition (jñāna) down to the precise relation by which each of its qualifying elements is connected to its substrate. The school's technical language is so distinctive that later Indian philosophy across all schools — including, eventually, Advaita Vedānta and the various Tantric philosophical traditions — adopts Navya-Nyāya's analytical vocabulary as a kind of common formal notation for stating philosophical positions with maximal precision, regardless of the substantive doctrines being defended.

15.2 The Qualifier-Qualificand Analysis: A Worked Example

Where the classical Nyāya inference (Section VII) states simply that "the hill has fire because it has smoke," Navya-Nyāya analyses the cognition underlying this inference into its full relational structure. The cognition's qualificandum (viśeṣya) is the hill; the hill is qualified (viśiṣṭa) by fire-possession-ness (vahnimattva), an abstract property formed from "fire" by the addition of the abstract suffix; the relation (saṃsarga) by which fire-possession-ness qualifies the hill is contact (saṃyoga), since fire is conjoined with the hill rather than inhering in it as a quality inheres in a substance; and the entire qualification is further delimited (avacchinna) by a specification of which particular fire is meant and by virtue of which evidentiary ground (the smoke) the qualification is known rather than merely supposed. Stating the inference's underlying cognition with this degree of explicit relational structure allows Navya-Nyāya to distinguish, with complete precision, between cognitions that ordinary language would express identically but that differ in their underlying logical form — a distinction whose absence, the school holds, is the single most common source of fallacious reasoning in informally conducted philosophical debate.

15.3 Why Hyper-Precision: The Problem of Ekārtha-Samavāya

The school's drive toward ever-greater relational precision is not scholastic indulgence but a response to a specific recurring problem the classical Nyāya inference-structure (Section VII) leaves underspecified: the problem of ekārtha-samavāya, the inherence of multiple distinct properties in a single substrate, and the consequent risk of conflating which property is doing the inferential work in a given case. Two properties may both be true of the same hill — its possession of fire and, say, its possession of a particular shade of grey rock — without the second property bearing any inferential relevance to the conclusion being drawn; ordinary Sanskrit sentence-structure does not, by itself, distinguish the inferentially load-bearing qualification from the merely co-present one, and a debater wishing to mislead an opponent (recall jāti, futile objection, from Section VI's sixteenth padārtha) can exploit exactly this ambiguity. Navya-Nyāya's avacchedaka-vocabulary exists precisely to close this loophole by making explicit, for every qualification asserted, the specific delimiting property by virtue of which the qualification holds.

15.4 Why This Matters for the Citta-Discrimination Argument

Navya-Nyāya's hyper-precise relational vocabulary is the tradition's most extreme instance of the citta-requirement this paper's Section 2.3 identifies for grammatical rule-application: a Navya-Nyāya text cannot be read at all, let alone applied, without a citta capable of holding several nested levels of qualifier-qualificand and relation-relata structure simultaneously active and correctly distinguished, often across a single, deliberately compressed Sanskrit sentence whose surface brevity conceals an analytical structure of considerable depth. The school's critics have sometimes treated this as scholastic over-elaboration disconnected from any substantive philosophical payoff; from the series' perspective, Navya-Nyāya is better understood as the logical tradition's own pursuit of the same ideal Pāṇini pursued in the grammatical domain — the construction of a metalanguage so precise that no ambiguity of scope or reference can survive correctly disciplined application — carried to its furthest extension, and therefore as a second, independent confirmation of the complementarity thesis (Section 10.1): formal precision of this order is not separable from the sustained, concentrated discipline of the cognising citta that the Yoga-śāstric tradition describes, since the relational distinctions Navya-Nyāya specifies must be actively, simultaneously held in mind by any practitioner applying them, not merely consulted as an external reference.

XVI.

Pratyabhijñā and Vyākaraṇa: Kashmir Śaivism's Re-Reading of Sphoṭa

Section Overview — This section reconstructs the Kashmir Śaiva Pratyabhijñā tradition's appropriation of Bhartṛhari's Vāk-philosophy, develops the re-grounding of the four Vāk-levels within a non-dual metaphysics of consciousness, and connects this appropriation to the series' own structural argument across its twelve parts.

16.1 Vāk as Śakti: The Trika Appropriation of the Grammatical Tradition

The Kashmir Śaiva Pratyabhijñā tradition, particularly in the work of Utpaladeva (tenth century CE) and his great commentator Abhinavagupta (late tenth to early eleventh century CE), draws extensively on Bhartṛhari's sphoṭa-theory and Vāk-philosophy but re-grounds it within a non-dual Śaiva metaphysics in which the four Vāk-levels (parā, paśyantī, madhyamā, vaikharī — recalled from Section 4.2) are not merely stages of speech-production internal to an individual speaker's cognitive process but stages in the self-manifestation of consciousness itself (citi, also called vimarśa, reflective self-awareness) as the entire manifest universe. On this re-reading, the parā level is identified directly with Parā Vāk as the Goddess — the supreme creative power (svātantrya-śakti, the freedom-power) of Śiva-consciousness — and the grammatical tradition's careful analysis of how meaning descends from undifferentiated speech-intention through paśyantī's "seeing" stage and madhyamā's internally structured stage to vaikharī's externalised, differentiated utterance becomes, for the Pratyabhijñā philosophers, a precise technical map of how the absolute itself differentiates into the manifest world of subjects and objects without ever ceasing to be one undivided awareness.

16.2 Abhinavagupta's Technical Borrowing: Sphoṭa as Cosmogonic Principle

Abhinavagupta's Tantrāloka and his commentarial works on the Pratyabhijñā-Kārikā repeatedly employ grammatical technical vocabulary — sphoṭa, pratibhā, vimarśa as a near-synonym of pratibhā's flash-quality — not as loose metaphor but as precisely borrowed technical apparatus repurposed for a cosmogonic rather than merely linguistic explanatory task. Just as Bhartṛhari's sphoṭa is the stable, unitary, meaning-bearing entity that the temporally extended sequence of phonemes reveals without being identical to it (Section 4.1), so too, on the Pratyabhijñā account, is the entire manifest universe of differentiated objects and subjects the "revealed" expression of an underlying unitary consciousness that the universe's apparent multiplicity never actually divides or diminishes. The technical precision of the borrowing is itself significant: Abhinavagupta does not merely gesture at an analogy between language and cosmos but works through the grammatical tradition's actual analytical categories — including, in places, explicit citation of specific Vākyapadīya passages — to construct his metaphysical argument, treating Bhartṛhari's grammatical philosophy as already, in effect, a covert metaphysics requiring only its non-dual Śaiva re-interpretation to be made explicit.

16.3 The Significance for This Series' Trajectory

This appropriation is directly relevant to the series' own structure, since Part One's foundational Vāk-account already drew on this same Trika reading in establishing the four-level schema this entire series has employed since its opening pages. This section's argument is that vyākaraṇa's technical achievement (Pāṇini's systematic grammar and Bhartṛhari's sphoṭa-philosophy) and Kashmir Śaivism's metaphysical achievement (the Pratyabhijñā account of self-recognising consciousness) are not two unrelated borrowings of a shared vocabulary occurring independently in two separate intellectual lineages, but a single intellectual lineage in which grammatical technicality becomes, without any change of its formal content, a vocabulary adequate to describe the self-differentiation of consciousness — confirming, from yet another direction and across yet another several centuries of textual transmission, this paper's recurring claim that the grammatical tradition's discriminative precision and the contemplative tradition's account of awareness are convergent achievements of a single broader intellectual culture rather than separate or merely coincidentally overlapping enterprises.

XVII.

Computational and Generative-Linguistic Resonances

Section Overview — This section examines the formal-structural resonances between the Aṣṭādhyāyī's architecture and modern generative and computational linguistics, surveys the principal scholarly literature establishing these resonances, and identifies the precise point at which the analogy reaches its limit.

17.1 The Aṣṭādhyāyī as a Formal Rewrite System

Modern computational linguistics has repeatedly recognised in the Aṣṭādhyāyī's architecture (Section II) a structure formally analogous to a context-sensitive rewrite system or finite-state transducer cascade: an ordered set of rules, each specifying a structural-description-to-structural-change mapping, applied in a determinate sequence with explicit conflict-resolution principles. The apavāda/utsarga blocking-principle of Section 3.2 corresponds closely to the "elsewhere condition" that twentieth-century generative phonology independently arrived at for resolving competing rule-applications; the anuvṛtti-mechanism's formal counterpart is the persistent-feature mechanism employed by many rule-based parsing formalisms, in which a feature specified at one point in a derivation remains active across subsequent derivational steps until explicitly overwritten. This is not merely a loose analogy of convenience drawn after the fact by modern scholars eager to find historical precedent for their own formalisms; several twentieth-century formal linguists, most notably Paul Kiparsky, have used Pāṇinian principles as direct technical resources in the development of generative phonological theory, treating the Aṣṭādhyāyī as a genuine and still-instructive precedent for the formal architecture of rule-ordering.

17.2 The Pratyāhāra System and Modern Feature-Geometry

A second, more specific resonance obtains between the Śiva-sūtras' pratyāhāra-system (Section 2.2) and the natural-class machinery of modern distinctive-feature phonology. Both systems exist to solve the same formal problem: how to refer economically to a phonologically natural class of sounds — a class that recurs as the domain of multiple distinct phonological rules — without listing its members individually on each occasion of reference. The pratyāhāra-system solves this through an ordered linear arrangement and a two-syllable naming convention; modern feature-geometry solves it through a hierarchically organised set of binary or multi-valued distinctive features, such that a natural class is picked out by specifying a small set of feature-values shared by all and only the class's members. That two independently developed formal systems, separated by roughly two and a half millennia and arising from entirely unrelated intellectual traditions, converge on solving the same underlying problem — economical reference to phonologically natural classes — is taken by several scholars in the Pāṇinian-studies literature as independent confirmation that the classes the pratyāhāras name are not artefacts of Pāṇini's particular notational choices but genuine, language-independently discoverable structural facts about how phonological systems are organised.

17.3 Where the Analogy Breaks: Pratibhā and the Limits of Formalisation

The analogy's limit is philosophically instructive rather than merely a caveat appended for completeness. A rewrite-system can, given sufficient rules correctly ordered and correctly specified, generate well-formed strings; what it cannot, on its own formal resources alone, produce is the holistic apprehension of sentence-meaning that Bhartṛhari's pratibhā (Section 5.2) names. A generative grammar of the Pāṇinian or the modern computational type specifies, with complete formal precision, which strings are well-formed; it does not, and on its own terms cannot, specify what it is for a hearer to understand one of those strings, in the sense of grasping its meaning as a unified, immediately apprehended whole rather than merely registering that it satisfies the grammar's well-formedness conditions. The Navya-Nyāya analysis of cognition's relational structure (Section 15.2) likewise formalises the structure of a fully articulated cognition with great precision without thereby formalising the cognising event itself — the citta's actual act of apprehending that structure as a unified content. The series' recurring claim that formal-technical mastery and the trained citta's capacity for direct apprehension are complementary rather than reducible to one another (Section 10.1) is, in this section's terms, the claim that no purely computational reconstruction of vyākaraṇa or nyāya — however successful as formal linguistics or as formal logic in its own right — captures the discipline's full traditional self-understanding, which always locates the rule-system's proper functioning within a disciplined, attentive, truth-seeking cognising subject rather than treating the rule-system as a self-sufficient, subject-independent mechanism.

XVIII.

Vaiśeṣika Categories and the Padārtha-Method's Wider Application

Section Overview — This section reconstructs Kaṇāda's Vaiśeṣika categorial scheme, examines its eventual synthesis with Nyāya, and develops the samavāya-relation's structural parallel to the sphoṭa-problem addressed in Section IV.

18.1 The Six (Later Seven) Padārthas of Kaṇāda

Nyāya's sixteen padārthas (Section VI) are frequently studied alongside the related but independently developed categorial scheme of the Vaiśeṣika school, founded on Kaṇāda's Vaiśeṣika-Sūtra (likely composed in stages between roughly the second century BCE and the early centuries CE). Where Nyāya's padārthas are a taxonomy of the elements of valid argument and inquiry (Section 6.1), Vaiśeṣika's padārthas are a taxonomy of the basic ontological furniture of reality itself: substance (dravya, of which there are nine kinds — earth, water, fire, air, ether, time, space, self, and mind); quality (guṇa, originally seventeen and later expanded to twenty-four); action (karma, of five kinds); universal (sāmānya); particularity (viśeṣa, the principle individuating otherwise qualitatively identical atoms); inherence (samavāya); and — added by later Vaiśeṣikas, notably Śrīdhara and Udayana, as a seventh category — absence (abhāva, itself subdivided into prior absence, posterior absence, mutual absence, and absolute absence).

18.2 Atomism and the Problem of Composite Wholes

Vaiśeṣika physics is atomistic: the four material elements (earth, water, fire, air) are held to be ultimately composed of indivisible, eternal atoms (paramāṇu) that combine, first in pairs (dvyaṇuka) and then in larger aggregates, to produce the perceptible composite substances of ordinary experience. This atomism generates, as a direct consequence, the school's most philosophically demanding technical problem: the relationship between a composite whole and its constituent parts. A cloth is composed of threads, a pot of clay-particles, a word — on the analogy this section draws out — of phonemes; in each case, the whole is perceived and referred to as a single unified thing, yet at the level of its physical constituents it is, strictly, a plurality. The Vaiśeṣika's solution is to hold that the whole (avayavin) is a distinct, additional entity over and above its parts (avayava), related to those parts neither by identity nor by mere external juxtaposition but by the unique, irreducible relation of samavāya (inherence).

18.3 Samavāya as the Vaiśeṣika Counterpart of Sphoṭa

The Vaiśeṣika category of samavāya thus addresses, from the ontological side, a problem structurally analogous to the sphoṭa-problem (Section 4.1) addressed from the linguistic side: how a composite, temporally or spatially extended entity can be apprehended and referred to as a single unified thing despite being, at the level of its physical constituents, a plurality. The Vaiśeṣika's answer — that the whole is a distinct, additional entity over and above its parts, related to them by samavāya — and the grammarian's answer — that the sphoṭa is a distinct, additional entity over and above the sequence of phonemes that reveals it, related to that sequence by the relation of manifestation (abhivyakti) rather than samavāya specifically — are independent solutions to a shared structural puzzle about part-whole relations and the unity of composite objects of cognition, each developed within its own śāstra's technical vocabulary and each defended against its own school's characteristic objections. The structural parallel was not lost on the classical tradition itself: later syncretic Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika authors explicitly debate whether sphoṭa ought to be analysed as a species of avayavin (composite whole) governed by ordinary samavāya, a position the strict grammarian resists precisely because it would assimilate the sphoṭa's distinctive manifestation-relation to the ontologically more general inherence-relation, thereby losing what the grammarian takes to be the sphoṭa's distinctive explanatory role.

18.4 The Synthesis into Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika

The eventual fusion of the two schools into the syncretic Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika tradition — already reflected in this paper's bibliography at Footnote 6, and standard in nearly all post-tenth-century Nyāya literature — reflects the tradition's own recognition that a complete account of valid knowledge-production (Nyāya's contribution: the pramāṇas, the avayavas, the hetvābhāsas) requires a determinate account of what there is to be known (Vaiśeṣika's contribution: the categorial inventory of substance, quality, action, universal, particularity, inherence, and absence). Neither discipline, on its own technical resources, is complete: Nyāya's epistemology presupposes some account of the objects epistemic processes operate on, and Vaiśeṣika's ontology presupposes some account of how its categorial claims are themselves known to be valid. The synthesis is thus not an accidental historical merger of two originally separate schools but a structurally motivated completion of each discipline's own internal requirements.

XIX.

Pedagogical Method: How the Gurukula Actually Trained the Discriminating Citta

Section Overview — This section reconstructs the actual sequence of traditional vyākaraṇa and nyāya pedagogy, develops the two disciplines' contrasting acquisition-structures, and examines the institutional apprenticeship-model nyāya training specifically requires.

19.1 Stages of Aṣṭādhyāyī-Acquisition

Traditional vyākaraṇa pedagogy, as preserved in the still-living ghaṭikā and pāṭhaśālā systems of Sanskrit education across South India and elsewhere on the subcontinent, proceeds through a sequence designed precisely to build the simultaneous multi-element working-memory capacity this paper's Section 2.3 identifies as the Aṣṭādhyāyī's citta-requirement. The student first memorises a topically rearranged digest — most commonly Bhaṭṭoji Dīkṣita's Siddhānta-Kaumudī (seventeenth century CE) or one of its derivative simplified versions such as the Laghu-Kaumudī or Madhya-Kaumudī — since the Aṣṭādhyāyī's own sequence, optimised for anuvṛtti-economy rather than pedagogical clarity, is acquisition-hostile if attempted as a first approach: a beginning student encountering the bare sūtra-sequence cannot yet see which sūtras govern which morphological category, because that information is distributed and compressed across the text in a way that only becomes transparent once the relevant categories are already independently familiar. Only after the topical digest is secure — typically several years into a traditional course of study — does the student return to the Aṣṭādhyāyī's own sequence and begin tracing, sūtra by sūtra, the anuvṛtti-chains and adhikāra-domains in their original, maximally compressed form.

19.2 The Two-Stage Method as Pedagogical Instantiation of Apavāda/Utsarga

This two-stage method — topical clarity before economical compression — is itself a pedagogical instantiation of the apavāda/utsarga principle developed in Section 3.2: the general topical map (functioning here as the pedagogical utsarga, the broad orienting framework) must be securely held before the specific, highly compressed rule-order (functioning here as a kind of apavāda relative to pedagogical ease, since it refines and complicates the topical map's broad categories into their full technical precision) can be productively engaged. A student who attempted the reverse order — beginning with the Aṣṭādhyāyī's own compressed sequence before any topical orientation — would face exactly the kind of overgeneration-undergeneration confusion (Section 13.3) that the vārtika-literature exists to correct at the level of the rule-system itself, but now reproduced at the level of the individual learner's incomplete grasp of that system.

19.3 Memorisation, Recitation, and the Formation of Stable Saṃskāras

Both stages of traditional vyākaraṇa-acquisition rely heavily on memorisation through structured oral recitation, a method whose cognitive function extends well beyond simple information-storage. Daily recitation of memorised sūtra-sequences, continued over years, deposits the relevant rule-structures as the kind of stable saṃskāras — automatic, reliable cognitive habits — that Part Six's account of saṃskāra-formation describes as the product of sustained practice (abhyāsa). The expert grammarian's capacity to apply a relevant rule instantaneously and correctly to a novel linguistic form, without consciously working through the rule-system's full derivational chain on each occasion, is the direct product of this extended memorisation-and-recitation training, and constitutes the linguistic-domain instance of precisely the automatised discriminative reliability this paper's earlier sections (especially Section 2.3) describe as the goal of grammatical training.

19.4 Nyāya Pedagogy: The Dialectical Apprenticeship

Nyāya training proceeds differently, because its target competence — the vāda-capable citta described in Section 9.2 — is irreducibly dialogical rather than purely textual. A student cannot acquire reliable inference-construction and fallacy-detection skill by solitary memorisation alone, however extensive; the skill must be progressively built through actual exposure to live or carefully simulated philosophical exchange. Traditional nyāya pedagogy accordingly proceeds in three further stages, additional to and building upon the textual study of the Nyāya-Sūtra, Vātsyāyana's Bhāṣya, and the Navya-Nyāya literature of Section XV: first, the student observes senior debates conducted by qualified paṇḍitas, learning to recognise correctly formed five-membered inferences (Section VII) and to identify, in real time, the hetvābhāsas, chala, and jāti (Section VI) that an opposing party may attempt to introduce; second, the student is required to take the respondent's role in structured exchanges defending stock positions whose correct defence is already established in the literature, building familiarity with the rhythm and pressure of live dialectical exchange under conditions where the correct outcome is known in advance and can be checked; and third, the student is required to construct and defend an original five-membered inference on a genuinely contested question, under real-time challenge from a qualified opponent, with no prior guarantee of the correct outcome — the stage at which independent nyāya-competence is finally certified.

19.5 The Institutional Necessity of Apprenticeship

This apprenticeship structure explains why the institutional frameworks of Section 9.1 — the gurukula and the debate-hall (śāstrārtha) specifically — are not incidental delivery-mechanisms for an otherwise purely textual discipline but constitutive parts of nyāya-competence's actual formation, in a way that has no precise analogue in the more text-internal pedagogy of vyākaraṇa described in Sections 19.1 through 19.3. A vyākaraṇa-student could, in principle, achieve full textual mastery through sufficiently extensive solitary study of the trimuni-vyākaraṇa (Section XIII) and its later commentarial literature, even if such solitary study is rarely how the discipline is in fact transmitted. A nyāya-student cannot achieve the corresponding mastery by textual study alone, because the discipline's target competence is defined in terms of performance under live dialectical pressure — a performance-condition that no amount of solitary textual study can, on its own, certify or even adequately train.

XX.

Synthesis: The Two Disciplines as a Single Discriminative Apparatus

Section Overview — This closing section recapitulates the seven convergences developed across Sections XIII–XIX, advances a stronger version of the paper's complementarity thesis, and specifies the precise sense in which vyākaraṇa and nyāya constitute a single underlying discriminative capacity rather than two merely cooperating disciplines.

20.1 Recapitulating the Convergences Across Sections XIII–XIX

The seven preceding deep-dive sections converge, from seven independent directions, on a single, reinforced version of this paper's central complementarity thesis (Section 10.1). The commentarial architecture of Section XIII shows discriminative precision accumulating across successive generations of trained cittas rather than residing, fully formed, in any single one — Pāṇini's, Kātyāyana's, and Patañjali's respective contributions each refining what the prior layer left imperfectly resolved. The Mīmāṃsā debate of Section XIV shows that even the prior question of what stability and identity in language consist in is itself adjudicated through rigorous inferential and grammatical argument across rival schools, rather than stipulated by either side as a starting assumption immune to challenge. Navya-Nyāya's hyper-formalisation in Section XV shows the logical tradition pursuing the grammatical tradition's own ideal of exhaustive technical precision to its furthest extreme, developing a relational vocabulary whose scope-disambiguating function directly parallels the grammatical tradition's own apavāda/utsarga and anuvṛtti-mechanisms. The Pratyabhijñā appropriation of Section XVI shows that this technical precision is, in the tradition's own later self-understanding, directly continuous with its most exalted metaphysical claims about the nature of awareness, the grammatical categories proving adequate, without alteration of their formal content, to articulate a cosmogony of consciousness. The computational resonances of Section XVII show the formal architecture's genuine, independently checkable rigour — confirmed by its convergence with modern generative linguistics arrived at through entirely unrelated historical routes — while also showing precisely where pratibhā exceeds any rewrite-system's formal reach. The Vaiśeṣika parallel of Section XVIII shows the same part-whole puzzle that motivates sphoṭa-theory recurring, independently and rigorously solved, in the tradition's ontological vocabulary through the samavāya-relation. And the pedagogical reconstruction of Section XIX shows that none of this technical apparatus, however refined at the textual level, is acquired or sustained except through institutionally embedded, multi-stage training of exactly the kind Part Six's account of saṃskāra-formation describes in the explicitly yogic domain.

20.2 The Single Discriminative Apparatus: A Stronger Formulation

Taken together, these seven convergences support a stronger formulation of this paper's argument than Section 10.1's original statement allowed. The original complementarity thesis held that vyākaraṇa and nyāya are two complementary disciplines that happen to require a yogically-formed citta as their common precondition — a formulation that still treats the two disciplines as substantively independent achievements merely sharing a common cognitive prerequisite. The stronger formulation advanced here holds instead that vyākaraṇa and nyāya are two specialised functional modes of what is, at the level of the underlying cognitive capacity they jointly train and jointly draw upon, a single discriminative apparatus: one mode oriented toward the correct formulation of knowledge-claims in linguistically well-formed, technically precise expression (vyākaraṇa's domain, as developed across Sections II–V and XIII–XIV above), the other oriented toward the correct epistemic validation of those claims through rigorously specified inferential and categorial analysis (nyāya's domain, as developed across Sections VI–IX and XV, XVIII above) — both modes drawing on, and in turn further refining through their own technical elaboration, the same trained citta's capacity for sustained, multi-element, non-confused discrimination that Part Six identified as viveka-khyāti's distant but structurally related cognitive cousin.

20.3 Evidence for the Stronger Formulation: Cross-Disciplinary Borrowing

The strongest evidence for this stronger formulation is the extensive cross-disciplinary technical borrowing the preceding sections have documented in both directions. Nyāya does not merely happen to require grammatical literacy as an external prerequisite for stating its inferences in well-formed Sanskrit; Navya-Nyāya's own most distinctive technical innovation, the avacchedaka/viśeṣaṇa relational apparatus of Section 15.2, is constructed substantially through the same abstraction-forming morphological resources (the -tā/-tva suffixation system) that vyākaraṇa itself supplies and analyses, such that advanced nyāya-technicality is, at the morphological level, continuous with advanced grammatical technicality rather than merely dependent on a more basic grammatical competence. Conversely, vyākaraṇa's own philosophical self-understanding, as Sections IV, V, and XIV have shown, repeatedly requires properly inferential and epistemological argument — Bhartṛhari's defence of sphoṭa against the Mīmāṃsaka's saṃskāra-account proceeds by constructing and evaluating rival explanatory hypotheses according to broadly nyāya-style standards of argumentative adequacy, even though the substantive question at issue (what a word is in itself) is squarely a grammatical-philosophical one. This bidirectional technical interdependence — grammar requiring inferential argument to defend its philosophical claims, logic requiring grammatical resources to construct its most precise technical vocabulary — is difficult to explain on the weaker, merely-complementary formulation, but is precisely what the stronger, single-apparatus formulation predicts.

20.4 Implications for Part Eight and Beyond

This stronger formulation is what Part Eight will need. An applied śāstra like Arthaśāstra or Āyurveda does not invoke "grammatical skill" and "logical skill" as two separate imported toolkits, borrowed wholesale from vyākaraṇa and nyāya respectively and applied side by side without further integration; it draws on this single underlying discriminative apparatus, redirected in its entirety toward the specific subject-matters — statecraft's domain of political and economic organisation, Āyurveda's domain of the body's constitution and health — that Part Seven's three limits (Section XI) identified as currently unaddressed by either discipline on its own. The Arthaśāstra's own technical vocabulary, as Part Eight will show, borrows as freely from nyāya's inferential apparatus (in its analysis of statecraft's characteristic problems of evidence, testimony, and decision under uncertainty) as it does from vyākaraṇa's precision of definition and category (in its systematic taxonomies of administrative function and revenue-category) — confirming, before Part Eight has even properly begun, that the single discriminative apparatus this section identifies is genuinely portable across the tradition's full range of applied disciplines, rather than an artefact specific to the linguistic and logical domains in which it was first and most extensively developed.

Mīmāṃsā Hermeneutics: Vidhi, Tarka, and the Logic of Ritual Interpretation

21.1 Dimension One — Textual-Historical Foundation

The Mīmāṃsā school's foundational text is Jaimini's Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra (likely compiled in stages between roughly the third century BCE and the early centuries CE), with Śabara's Bhāṣya (c. fifth century CE) as its primary authorised commentary and Kumārila Bhaṭṭa's Ślokavārtika and Tantravārtika (seventh century CE) as the school's most philosophically developed elaboration. Already introduced in Section XIV as the grammatical tradition's rival on the question of sphoṭa, the Mīmāṃsā school's primary and founding concern is in fact prior to and independent of that debate: the systematic interpretation of Vedic ritual injunction (vidhi), addressing what a given Vedic sentence commands, to whom, under what conditions, and toward what result.

21.2 Dimension Two — Technical-Formal Architecture

Mīmāṃsā develops its own distinct technical apparatus for ritual-textual interpretation, substantially independent of vyākaraṇa's grammatical metalanguage and nyāya's inferential metalanguage, though drawing on both where relevant. Central technical devices include: śruti, liṅga, vākya, prakaraṇa, sthāna, and samākhyā — the six recognised sources of interpretive authority, ranked in a strict order of priority such that a more direct textual indicator always overrides a less direct one in cases of apparent conflict; the adhikaraṇa-method, a standard six-part analytical format (viṣaya, saṃśaya, pūrvapakṣa, uttarapakṣa/siddhānta, saṅgati, with a final restatement) through which every interpretive problem in the Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra is presented and resolved; and the doctrine of arthāpatti (postulation), a fifth pramāṇa recognised by Mīmāṃsā but not by Nyāya (Section VIII), whereby a fact not directly perceived, inferred, compared, or testified to is nonetheless validly known because some otherwise inexplicable known fact requires it as its only adequate explanation.

21.3 Dimension Three — Philosophical Implications

The adhikaraṇa-method's strict five-step format — statement of the topic, articulation of the doubt that motivates inquiry, statement of the prima facie position with its supporting reasoning, statement and defence of the settled conclusion, and demonstration of the topic's coherence with its surrounding textual context — is philosophically significant beyond its immediate exegetical function: it is, in effect, Mīmāṃsā's own formalisation of the structure every act of textual interpretation already implicitly follows, made explicit and rendered checkable. The deeper philosophical claim embedded in the method is that textual meaning is never simply given on a single reading but must be argued for against a genuine, fairly stated rival position — a methodological commitment that aligns Mīmāṃsā hermeneutics with the nyāya-tradition's insistence (Section IX, vāda versus jalpa) that knowledge-claims achieve their proper epistemic standing only through survival of genuine dialectical challenge, not through unopposed assertion.

21.4 Dimension Four — Comparative / Cross-Śāstric Resonance

The adhikaraṇa-method's five-step structure bears a structural resemblance, not incidental, to the nyāya five-membered inference (avayava, Section VII): both are five-part formal sequences in which a thesis is stated, contested, and then re-established through an explicit closing step that restates the original claim now as settled knowledge. The resemblance is unsurprising given the extensive historical interaction between the two schools — Mīmāṃsā draws on developed nyāya inferential technique throughout its interpretive arguments, while nyāya's own treatment of śabda as a pramāṇa (Section 8.2) draws extensively on Mīmāṃsā's prior, more detailed analysis of how testimonial language generates valid knowledge. The arthāpatti-pramāṇa, meanwhile, stands as Mīmāṃsā's most original epistemological contribution beyond what either vyākaraṇa or nyāya independently supplies, addressing a class of valid inference-like reasoning (reasoning from an otherwise inexplicable fact to its necessary explanatory condition) that the strict nyāya account of anumāna's vyāpti-requirement (Section 7.2) does not straightforwardly capture.

21.5 Dimension Five — Citta-Pedagogical Significance

Competent adhikaraṇa-construction requires a citta capable of genuinely inhabiting, and stating with full argumentative force, a position the practitioner does not in the end hold — the pūrvapakṣa, or prima facie view, must be constructed as the strongest version of the position to be rejected, not as a straw-man easily dismissed, since the adhikaraṇa's persuasive force depends entirely on the settled conclusion's having survived a genuine rather than a merely staged challenge. This is a distinct and demanding citta-capacity, related to but not identical with the nyāya-debater's capacity (Section 19.4) to respond to a live opponent's actual position: the Mīmāṃsā exegete must construct that opposing position single-handedly, with the same rigour a genuine opponent would bring, before proceeding to dismantle it — a discipline of intellectual honesty toward positions one will ultimately reject that the tradition regards as indispensable to interpretive integrity.

XXII.

Alaṅkāraśāstra: Dhvani-Theory and the Grammar-Poetics Interface

22.1 Dimension One — Textual-Historical Foundation

Alaṅkāraśāstra, the science of poetic ornamentation and literary criticism, reaches its most philosophically consequential development in Ānandavardhana's Dhvanyāloka (ninth century CE), with Abhinavagupta's Locana (already encountered in Section XVI as the Pratyabhijñā philosopher) as its most authoritative commentary. The Dhvanyāloka's central doctrine, dhvani (literally "resonance" or "suggestion"), proposes that a poem's deepest aesthetic meaning — most paradigmatically the rasa, the aesthetic emotion already developed at length in this series' Part Four — is not stated directly by any of the poem's words but is suggested (vyañjita) by them through a third level of linguistic function operating beyond the two functions vyākaraṇa and nyāya had already analysed.

22.2 Dimension Two — Technical-Formal Architecture

Ānandavardhana's technical apparatus distinguishes three distinct functions (vṛtti) of a word: abhidhā, the primary, direct denotative function already presupposed throughout vyākaraṇa's account of grammatical meaning (Sections II–V); lakṣaṇā, secondary or figurative usage, in which a word's primary sense is set aside in favour of a related sense connected to it by some recognised relation (already a minor technical category within both vyākaraṇa and Mīmāṃsā, since both disciplines must occasionally explain non-literal Vedic and ordinary usage); and, as the Dhvanyāloka's distinctive innovation, vyañjanā, the suggestive function, by which a word or sentence, beyond and in addition to whatever it directly denotes or figuratively indicates, evokes a further sense — most importantly, a rasa — that cannot be reduced to either of the first two functions without explanatory loss.

22.3 Dimension Three — Philosophical Implications

The vyañjanā-doctrine's deepest philosophical stake is its claim that a sentence's full meaning-potential exceeds what either vyākaraṇa's careful semantic analysis (abhidhā and lakṣaṇā together) or nyāya's careful inferential analysis (treating poetic statements, where relevant, simply as testimonial śabda) can capture. This is not a claim that grammatical and logical analysis are mistaken within their own proper domain, but a claim that their domain, however exhaustively analysed, is not the whole of language's meaning-bearing capacity — a claim with an evident affinity to Bhartṛhari's pratibhā (Section 5.2), since the dhvani-theorist's account of how a competent reader grasps a poem's suggested rasa is, like Bhartṛhari's account of sentence-comprehension, holistic and instantaneous rather than built up through any explicit inferential or lexical-decompositional process the reader could, even in principle, fully articulate.

22.4 Dimension Four — Comparative / Cross-Śāstric Resonance

Ānandavardhana explicitly positions dhvani-theory in relation to both vyākaraṇa and Mīmāṃsā's lakṣaṇā-doctrine, arguing at length that vyañjanā cannot be reduced to or assimilated within lakṣaṇā's existing technical category, however the latter is extended — a defence that proceeds, methodologically, exactly like a vārtika's diagnostic argument (Section 13.2): construct the case that the existing category, however generously stretched, fails to cover, and show that only a genuinely new category closes the gap. The dhvani-theorist's relationship to nyāya is similarly precise rather than dismissive: later alaṅkāra-theorists, particularly Mammaṭa in the Kāvyaprakāśa (eleventh century CE), recast portions of the dhvani-doctrine using explicitly Navya-Nyāya-influenced relational vocabulary (Section XV) to specify with greater precision exactly which textual features license a given suggested sense in a given context, again illustrating the bidirectional technical borrowing this paper's Section 20.3 identified between grammar and logic, now extended to a third allied discipline.

22.5 Dimension Five — Citta-Pedagogical Significance

The sahṛdaya — the "sympathetic-hearted" reader whose trained sensibility the entire dhvani-theory presupposes as poetry's proper audience — names a citta-capacity distinct from both the grammarian's rule-holding capacity (Section 2.3) and the logician's inference-checking capacity (Section 19.4): the capacity to receive a suggested aesthetic sense without the explicit, step-by-step processing either grammatical or logical competence requires, while nonetheless being able, when called upon, to give a fully articulate technical account of why a given passage succeeds in evoking the rasa it does. The sahṛdaya's training, accordingly, is neither purely intuitive nor purely technical but is, like the complementarity thesis's ideal practitioner (Section 10.1), the cultivated conjunction of immediate aesthetic receptivity with full critical-technical command — a third instance, alongside vyākaraṇa and nyāya, of the single discriminative apparatus this paper's Section 20.2 identifies, here redirected toward the specific evaluative task of literary judgment.

XXIII.

Dharmaśāstra: Mīmāṃsā-Nyāya Method in Legal and Ethical Interpretation

23.1 Dimension One — Textual-Historical Foundation

Dharmaśāstra, the tradition's body of literature on law, custom, and ethical duty, reaches its classical synthesis in texts such as the Manusmṛti (likely compiled in stages, c. second century BCE to second century CE) and receives its most systematic later commentarial elaboration in works such as Vijñāneśvara's Mitākṣarā (eleventh-twelfth century CE) and Jīmūtavāhana's Dāyabhāga, the two texts whose differing interpretive principles on inheritance came to define the two major regional schools of classical Hindu law.

23.2 Dimension Two — Technical-Formal Architecture

Dharmaśāstra's interpretive method is, to a substantial degree, an application of the Mīmāṃsā hermeneutic apparatus developed in Section XXI — the six sources of interpretive authority (Table 21.1), the adhikaraṇa-method's five-step structure, and the strict ranking of textual indicators — transferred from the domain of Vedic ritual injunction to the domain of dharma-texts' prescriptive and prohibitive statements. Where the two great commentarial schools diverge most sharply is precisely in their application of the Mīmāṃsā-derived priority-ranking to specific, contested questions of inheritance-law, illustrating that a shared technical apparatus does not by itself guarantee a single determinate outcome when applied to genuinely difficult interpretive cases — the apparatus narrows but does not always close the space of reasonable disagreement.

23.3 Dimension Three — Philosophical Implications

Dharmaśāstra's reliance on Mīmāṃsā method for an explicitly legal-ethical rather than ritual subject-matter carries a significant philosophical implication for the series' broader account of śāstric proliferation (Section 1.1): it shows that a discipline's technical interpretive apparatus, once sufficiently well-developed within its originating domain, becomes a genuinely portable resource, transferable to subject-matters quite distant from the one that motivated its original construction. The Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra's authors were concerned with Vedic ritual injunction specifically, not with inheritance-law; yet the interpretive apparatus they developed for the former proved adequate, with comparatively modest adaptation, to the latter — direct confirmatory evidence, from outside the linguistic-logical domain this paper has primarily examined, for exactly the kind of cross-disciplinary portability this paper's Section 20.4 anticipates for vyākaraṇa and nyāya's single discriminative apparatus in Part Eight.

23.4 Dimension Four — Comparative / Cross-Śāstric Resonance

Dharmaśāstric commentators also draw, somewhat more selectively than the Mīmāṃsakas themselves, on nyāya's inferential apparatus — particularly in resolving disputed questions of fact (such as questions of legitimate paternity or the genuineness of a disputed document) where the available evidence falls short of direct textual authority and must instead be assessed through ordinary inferential reasoning of the kind Section VII develops, subject to the same standards of vyāpti-adequacy (Section 7.2) that govern any other anumāna. The two great commentarial schools' divergence on inheritance illustrates, moreover, a phenomenon the Nyāya padārtha of vāda (Section 6.1's tenth padārtha) already anticipates: that sincere, methodologically rigorous interpreters applying a shared apparatus in good faith can nonetheless reach different settled conclusions, without either side's interpretive process thereby ceasing to count as vāda rather than jalpa.

23.5 Dimension Five — Citta-Pedagogical Significance

Training in Dharmaśāstra's interpretive method, as the surviving curricular evidence from the great commentarial schools' own training-lineages attests, presupposed substantial prior competence in precisely the Mīmāṃsā and nyāya disciplines this paper's earlier sections examine, confirming again that the discriminative apparatus those disciplines cultivate functions as general-purpose preparatory training for any subsequent application to a specific applied domain — legal, ritual, or otherwise — rather than as domain-specific technical knowledge that must be separately reconstructed for each new field of application.

XXIV.

Sāṅkhya Categories and the Grammatical Theory of Padārtha

24.1 Dimension One — Textual-Historical Foundation

The classical Sāṅkhya system, systematised in Īśvarakṛṣṇa's Sāṅkhya-Kārikā (c. fourth century CE) and presupposed throughout this series' Part Six account of the Yoga-śāstra's psychological framework, develops its own independent categorial scheme — the twenty-five tattvas, comprising puruṣa (consciousness) and the twenty-four evolutes of prakṛti (the three guṇas in their unmanifest and progressively manifest forms, the eleven indriyas, the five elements, and the antaḥkaraṇa's three components) — quite independently of either the grammatical padārtha-tradition or the Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika categorial scheme examined in Section XVIII.

24.2 Dimension Two — Technical-Formal Architecture

Sāṅkhya's twenty-five-tattva scheme is organised, like the Vaiśeṣika padārtha-list (Table 18.1), as an exhaustive categorial inventory claiming to capture the complete furniture of reality without remainder — but organised along a fundamentally different principle: not Vaiśeṣika's logical taxonomy of substance, quality, action, universal, particularity, inherence, and absence, but a causal-evolutionary sequence (pariṇāma) in which each tattva is derived as the evolutionary product of the tattva immediately prior to it, beginning from prakṛti's unmanifest equilibrium of the three guṇas and proceeding through buddhi, ahaṅkāra, manas, the five jñānendriyas and five karmendriyas, and finally the five gross elements.

24.3 Dimension Three — Philosophical Implications

That three independently developed classical Indian schools each felt the need to construct an exhaustive categorial inventory — whatever their differing organising principles — suggests that the padārtha-method itself (the construction of a complete, non-overlapping, jointly exhaustive list of fundamental categories as the necessary first step of any rigorous śāstric inquiry) is not a technique peculiar to logic or to ontology narrowly construed, but a general methodological commitment of classical Indian intellectual culture as such: the conviction that disciplined inquiry into any domain must begin by correctly enumerating that domain's basic elements, on pain of confusing categorially distinct things — precisely the viveka, discrimination, this entire paper has traced through its linguistic and logical instances.

24.4 Dimension Four — Comparative / Cross-Śāstric Resonance

The antaḥkaraṇa's three components within the Sāṅkhya scheme — buddhi (discriminative intellect), ahaṅkāra (ego-principle), and manas (the coordinating mind-faculty) — supply the very psychological vocabulary this paper has repeatedly drawn upon in describing the citta-requirements of grammatical and logical competence (Sections 2.3, 15.4, 19.3): buddhi's discriminative function is precisely what is being trained when a grammarian learns to apply the apavāda/utsarga principle correctly (Section 3.2), and the citta-vocabulary this entire Series B has used since Part Six is, at root, Sāṅkhya-Yoga vocabulary, here shown to be continuous with the Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika categorial tradition's prameya-padārtha (the second Nyāya category, Section 6.1, which explicitly lists buddhi, manas, and related psychological items among the objects of valid knowledge).

24.5 Dimension Five — Citta-Pedagogical Significance

Sāṅkhya's own account of how the twenty-five tattvas are correctly discriminated — culminating, as Part Six's Section X established, in viveka-khyāti, the direct discriminative apprehension of puruṣa's distinctness from all twenty-four evolutes of prakṛti — supplies the deepest psychological grounding this paper's entire argument has presupposed: the discriminative capacity vyākaraṇa and nyāya train at the linguistic and logical level is, on the Sāṅkhya-Yoga account this series has followed since Part Six, a specialised application of the same fundamental discriminative capacity (buddhi correctly distinguishing categorially distinct tattvas) whose fullest exercise is viveka-khyāti itself — confirming, from the psychological-metaphysical side rather than the technical-disciplinary side this paper has primarily examined, the single discriminative apparatus thesis of Section 20.2.

XXV.

Tantric Mātṛkā Theory: The Phoneme as Cosmological Principle

25.1 Dimension One — Textual-Historical Foundation

Tantric mātṛkā theory, developed across the Āgama and Tantra literature of the Śaiva and Śākta traditions and given its most systematic philosophical treatment in Abhinavagupta's Parātrīśikā-Vivaraṇa (already encountered in Section XVI as the principal Pratyabhijñā source for the appropriation of Bhartṛhari's Vāk-philosophy), treats the fifty phonemes of the Sanskrit alphabet — the mātṛkā, literally "little mothers" — not merely as the building-blocks of Sanskrit utterance but as the fundamental creative-cosmological powers (śaktis) from whose combination the entire manifest universe, not merely manifest language, is held to proceed.

25.2 Dimension Two — Technical-Formal Architecture

Mātṛkā theory's technical apparatus maps the fifty phonemes onto the cakra-system of subtle physiology, onto specific deities and creative functions, and onto the Śiva-sūtras' own fourteen pratyāhāra-defining phonemic groupings (Section 2.2) — the same fourteen groups Pāṇini uses for purely grammatical economy are read, in the Tantric context, as fourteen stages or aspects of the cosmos's phonemic self-differentiation. The cakra known as mātṛkā-nyāsa — the ritual installation of specific phonemes onto specific points of the practitioner's body — operationalises this mapping directly, treating the grammatical tradition's phonemic taxonomy as a literal map of the practitioner's own subtle constitution.

25.3 Dimension Three — Philosophical Implications

Mātṛkā theory thus extends the Pratyabhijñā appropriation already examined in Section XVI a further degree: not merely the four Vāk-levels (parā, paśyantī, madhyamā, vaikharī) but the Śiva-sūtras' specific fourteen-group phonemic taxonomy itself — a taxonomy this paper's Section 2.2 established as motivated by Pāṇini's purely formal-economical concerns — is taken up, without alteration of its internal structure, as adequate notation for a fully cosmological and soteriological doctrine. This double appropriation (the four-level schema and the fourteen-group taxonomy) is the strongest available evidence within the classical tradition itself for this paper's recurring claim that the grammatical tradition's technical categories possess a content sufficiently rich and sufficiently well-individuated to bear genuine metaphysical weight, rather than being merely convenient descriptive shorthand exhausted by its original narrow grammatical purpose.

25.4 Dimension Four — Comparative / Cross-Śāstric Resonance

Mātṛkā theory stands in a directly continuous relationship with the sphoṭa-theory of Section IV and the Pratyabhijñā material of Section XVI: where Bhartṛhari treats the sphoṭa as the stable meaning-bearing unit a phonemic sequence reveals, and Abhinavagupta's Pratyabhijñā treats the four Vāk-levels as stages of consciousness's self-manifestation, mātṛkā theory treats the fifty individual phonemes themselves as the most fundamental creative units from whose combinatorial play both sphoṭa-level word-units and the entire manifest cosmos alike proceed — a third, still more fundamental layer added beneath the two this paper has already examined, completing what the broader Cultural Musings research programme elsewhere (in its dedicated Śabda-Brahman thesis-series) treats as a unified Tantric grammatical-cosmological architecture.

25.5 Dimension Five — Citta-Pedagogical Significance

Mātṛkā-nyāsa practice requires of the practitioner's citta a capacity quite distinct from the purely intellectual rule-holding capacity Section 2.3 describes for grammatical competence: a sustained, embodied, ritually structured attention in which the grammatical-phonemic taxonomy is not merely cognitively held but somatically installed, phoneme by phoneme, at specific points of bodily awareness — a citta-discipline that bridges this series' Part Five account of embodied abhinaya-cognition and the present paper's account of grammatical-discriminative cognition, suggesting that the trained citta's capacity for precise, multi-element discrimination (this paper's recurring theme) extends, in the Tantric tradition's own self-understanding, beyond purely intellectual or purely linguistic application into directly somatic and ritual registers as well.

XXVI.

The Buddhist Interlocutor: Dignāga, Dharmakīrti, and Apoha-Theory

26.1 Dimension One — Textual-Historical Foundation

No account of classical Indian logic and philosophy of language is complete without its most formidable rival tradition: the Buddhist epistemological school founded by Dignāga (c. fifth century CE) in the Pramāṇasamuccaya and brought to its most rigorous systematic completion by Dharmakīrti (c. seventh century CE) in the Pramāṇavārttika. This school's engagement with both vyākaraṇa and nyāya was sustained, technically exacting, and — as Section 14.2's reference to Kumārila's anti-Buddhist polemical context already signalled — a major shaping pressure on the later development of both the grammatical and the Nyāya traditions themselves.

26.2 Dimension Two — Technical-Formal Architecture

Dignāga and Dharmakīrti reduce the Nyāya system's four pramāṇas (Section VIII) to two — pratyakṣa (perception) and anumāna (inference) alone, rejecting upamāna and śabda as independently valid sources of knowledge and instead analysing them as special cases of inference. Their most distinctive and influential technical contribution is apoha-theory, the doctrine that a general term's meaning is constituted not by reference to any positively existing universal (sāmānya, the Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika category of Section 18.1) but by exclusion (apoha) — a word like "cow" means, strictly, "not-non-cow," its apparent positive general meaning being constructed entirely through the systematic exclusion of everything the term does not apply to, rather than through positive reference to any really existing universal cow-ness.

26.3 Dimension Three — Philosophical Implications

Apoha-theory is the most direct possible challenge to the entire Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika categorial enterprise examined in Sections VI and XVIII: if there are no positively existing universals, then sāmānya (Section 18.1's fourth Vaiśeṣika padārtha) is not a genuine category of reality but a conceptual construction generated by language's own exclusionary mechanics, and a very large portion of the categorial apparatus this paper has examined as discovering the structure of reality is instead, on the Buddhist account, merely discovering the structure of language's own conceptual-constructive operations, projected illegitimately onto reality.

26.4 Dimension Four — Comparative / Cross-Śāstric Resonance

The Buddhist challenge forced both vyākaraṇa and Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika to sharpen their own positions considerably: later Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika authors, including the Navya-Nyāya tradition examined in Section XV, developed substantially more rigorous defences of sāmānya's reality precisely in response to apoha-theory's pressure, while the grammatical tradition's own jāti-discussions (the universal as a candidate primary meaning of a word, briefly noted in Section 5.1's account of Bhartṛhari's rival candidates for primary linguistic reference) were similarly sharpened through direct engagement with the Buddhist alternative. The historical pattern recurring throughout this paper — those who hold rival positions are nonetheless driven to ever-greater technical precision by the requirement of answering a genuinely formidable opponent — here finds its most consequential instance, the Buddhist-Brahmanical debate over universals occupying several centuries of the tradition's most sophisticated philosophical energy.

26.5 Dimension Five — Citta-Pedagogical Significance

Mastery of the Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika defence of sāmānya, in its mature post-Dharmakīrti form, requires a citta capable of holding not merely one's own positive doctrine in clear view (the ordinary requirement of any technical mastery this paper has examined) but the rival Buddhist apoha-account in equally clear and undistorted view, since the defence's entire argumentative force depends on showing precisely where the exclusion-based account fails to do the explanatory work a genuine universal does — a discipline of accurate opponent-representation closely related to, but more demanding than, the Mīmāṃsā pūrvapakṣa-construction examined in Section 21.5, since here the opposing tradition is not a constructed hypothetical position within one's own school but a fully developed, independently sophisticated rival philosophical system.

XXVII.

Advaita Vedānta's Selective Synthesis of Grammar and Logic

27.1 Dimension One — Textual-Historical Foundation

Advaita Vedānta, systematised by Śaṅkara (c. eighth century CE) in his commentaries on the Brahma-Sūtra, the principal Upaniṣads, and the Bhagavad-Gītā, and further technically elaborated by later authors including Vācaspati Miśra and Madhusūdana Sarasvatī, draws selectively but extensively on both the grammatical and the logical traditions this paper has examined, while subordinating both to its own non-dual metaphysical conclusions in a manner structurally comparable to, though doctrinally distinct from, the Kashmir Śaiva appropriation already examined in Section XVI.

27.2 Dimension Two — Technical-Formal Architecture

Śaṅkara's commentarial method draws directly on Mīmāṃsā's adhikaraṇa-structure (Section 21.2) for organising the Brahma-Sūtra's own difficult aphorisms into the same viṣaya-saṃśaya-pūrvapakṣa-siddhānta-saṅgati format, while his arguments for the reality of the non-dual Brahman and the ultimately unreal status of phenomenal multiplicity (the doctrine of adhyāsa, superimposition) draw on a precisely specified inferential apparatus recognisably indebted to nyāya's own techniques of hypothetical reasoning (tarka, Section 6.1's eighth padārtha) even while explicitly rejecting several of Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika's substantive realist commitments, particularly its commitment to the independent reality of the categorial multiplicity examined in Section XVIII.

27.3 Dimension Three — Philosophical Implications

Advaita's selective synthesis — adopting Mīmāṃsā's interpretive method and nyāya's inferential technique while rejecting Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika's realist metaphysics of independently existing categorial multiplicity — demonstrates a philosophically significant separability this paper has not yet explicitly drawn out: the technical-formal apparatus a discipline develops (its method of textual interpretation, its inferential technique) is, to a substantial degree, detachable from the specific substantive metaphysical conclusions that discipline's own founders happened to draw using that apparatus. Advaita's mahāvākya-analysis — its technical treatment of the great Upaniṣadic identity-statements such as tat tvam asi ("that thou art") — borrows Bhartṛhari's own sentence-meaning theory (Section 5.1's claim that the sentence rather than the word is the primary unit of meaning) in order to argue that such identity-statements convey their non-dual meaning through a special operation (lakṣaṇā, again, as in Section 22.2, though here applied to the entire mahāvākya rather than to an individual word) that yields direct, non-discursive realisation rather than merely propositional information.

27.4 Dimension Four — Comparative / Cross-Śāstric Resonance

Advaita's mahāvākya-vicāra (analysis of the great sentences) is thus a fourth instance, alongside the Mīmāṃsā hermeneutics of Section XXI, the alaṅkāra dhvani-theory of Section XXII, and the Pratyabhijñā appropriation of Section XVI, of vyākaraṇa's sentence-meaning theory being redeployed, with its formal content substantially intact, toward a further interpretive purpose its grammatical originators did not themselves intend — direct, further confirmation of this paper's recurring claim (most fully stated in Section 20.4) that the discriminative-interpretive apparatus vyākaraṇa and nyāya jointly develop functions as genuinely general-purpose technical resource, portable across an extraordinarily wide range of the tradition's later intellectual enterprises.

27.5 Dimension Five — Citta-Pedagogical Significance

Advaita pedagogy's traditional three-stage method — śravaṇa (hearing the mahāvākyas from a qualified teacher), manana (rigorous logical reflection upon them, removing doubt through exactly the kind of tarka-style hypothetical reasoning examined in Section 27.2), and nididhyāsana (sustained meditative assimilation) — explicitly requires the manana-stage's logical rigour as a necessary, though not sufficient, preparation for the immediate, non-discursive realisation nididhyāsana aims at: a three-stage structure that maps with some precision onto this paper's own recurring distinction between technical-discriminative mastery (vyākaraṇa's and nyāya's proper domain, corresponding to manana) and the holistic, non-discursive apprehension that technical mastery prepares for but cannot itself produce (pratibhā in Section 5.2's grammatical context, viveka-khyāti in Part Six's yogic context, and here brahma-jñāna in Advaita's own terms) — the clearest single instance in the classical tradition's own self-description of this paper's central complementarity thesis (Section 10.1) being explicitly thematised as a graduated pedagogical method.

XXVIII.

The Modern Reception: Colonial Scholarship, Revival, and Continuing Transmission

28.1 Dimension One — Textual-Historical Foundation

The modern scholarly encounter with the traditions this paper examines begins in earnest with the colonial-era European Indological recovery of Sanskrit grammatical and logical literature — Franz Bopp's and William Jones's foundational comparative-linguistic work drawing directly on Pāṇinian grammar from the late eighteenth century onward — and continues through the twentieth-century professionalisation of Pāṇinian studies (Cardona, Kiparsky, Joshi and Roodbergen, already cited throughout this paper's footnotes) and of classical Indian logic (Matilal, Ganeri, Potter), alongside a continuous, never-interrupted indigenous transmission through the traditional pāṭhaśālā system examined in Section XIX.

28.2 Dimension Two — Technical-Formal Architecture

The modern academic study of these disciplines has developed its own technical apparatus for representing and analysing the classical material — critical editions collating multiple manuscript traditions, systematic English technical glossaries for Sanskrit logical and grammatical terms (themselves a kind of modern vārtika-layer, in this paper's Section 13.2 sense, correcting and refining prior translators' imprecisions), and the comparative-formal methods examined in Section XVII that translate Pāṇinian and Navya-Nyāya structures into the notation of modern formal linguistics and logic.

28.3 Dimension Three — Philosophical Implications

The encounter between the indigenous pāṭhaśālā transmission (Section XIX) and the modern academic study of the same material has generated its own productive tension, structurally similar to several of the rival-school tensions this paper has examined elsewhere: the pāṭhaśālā-trained paṇḍita's competence is performance-certified through live śāstrārtha (Section 19.4) and is acquired through the saṃskāra-forming recitation-discipline of Section 19.3, while the modern academically trained scholar's competence is typically text-and-translation-based and certified through different institutional means (the doctoral dissertation, the peer-reviewed critical edition); each form of competence reveals capacities and limitations the other form does not straightforwardly share, and the most philologically and philosophically productive modern scholarship — including much of the work cited throughout this paper's footnotes — has typically arisen from sustained collaboration between the two.

28.4 Dimension Four — Comparative / Cross-Śāstric Resonance

This modern dual-transmission structure is itself a contemporary instance of the trans-generational, distributed discrimination this paper's Section 13.5 identifies in the trimuni-vyākaraṇa's three-layer commentarial architecture: just as Pāṇini, Kātyāyana, and Patañjali's successive contributions accumulated across historical time into a single refined system no individual generation produced alone, the modern critical-editorial and academic-analytical layer now stands, in relation to the millennia of prior commentarial accumulation this paper has traced (Sections XIII through XXVII), as a further, contemporary layer of exactly the same kind — correcting transmission-errors, clarifying ambiguous traditional interpretations through comparative manuscript evidence, and making the tradition's technical apparatus newly legible to audiences (including, now, the readers of this very series) who approach it without the pāṭhaśālā's specific multi-year training but with other relevant intellectual preparation.

28.5 Dimension Five — Citta-Pedagogical Significance

The Cultural Musings research programme's own working method — as this paper's Series B as a whole exemplifies — represents one further contemporary instance of the dual-transmission synthesis examined in Sections 28.3 and 28.4: a research practice that draws on primary Sanskrit sources in their original technical vocabulary (vyākaraṇa's pratyāhāras and anuvṛtti, Nyāya's padārthas and avayavas, the allied disciplines' own specific apparatus across Sections XXI–XXVII), on the full weight of modern critical-academic scholarship documented in this paper's footnotes, and on a sustained comparative engagement with modern scientific and computational frameworks (Section XVII) — itself, on this paper's own terms, best understood as a present-day exercise of the single discriminative apparatus (Section 20.2) this entire expanded treatment of Part Seven has traced across thirty-one centuries of continuous technical elaboration, from Pāṇini's fourth-century-BCE sūtras to the reader's own twenty-first-century engagement with this text.

Closing Synthesis of the Five-Dimensional Series

From Vyākaraṇa and Nyāya to the Tradition's Full Discriminative Range

Sections XXI through XXVIII, applying the consistent five-dimensional structure specified at this block's outset, have traced the same underlying discriminative apparatus this paper's Sections I–XX establish across eight further disciplines and rival traditions: Mīmāṃsā's ritual-interpretive hermeneutics (XXI), Alaṅkāraśāstra's poetics of suggestion (XXII), Dharmaśāstra's legal-ethical interpretation (XXIII), Sāṅkhya's categorial psychology (XXIV), Tantric mātṛkā-cosmology (XXV), the Buddhist epistemological challenge (XXVI), Advaita Vedānta's selective synthesis (XXVII), and the modern scholarly and continuing traditional reception of the entire corpus (XXVIII). In each case, the same five questions — textual-historical foundation, technical-formal architecture, philosophical implication, comparative resonance with vyākaraṇa and nyāya, and citta-pedagogical requirement — have revealed a discipline whose technical apparatus, however specific to its own subject-matter, remains continuous with, and in most cases directly derivative of, the grammatical and logical apparatus this paper's main body develops at length. The cumulative weight of these eight further confirmations is to extend this paper's complementarity thesis (Section 10.1) and its stronger single-apparatus formulation (Section 20.2) into a claim about the entire classical Indian intellectual tradition's structure: that what looks, from a distance, like a proliferation of many distinct śāstras and darśanas is, on sustained technical inspection, the many-textured expression of one sustained, continuously refined discriminative capacity — and that the cultivation of that capacity, across all its textual, technical, and citta-formational dimensions alike, is the tradition's single most enduring intellectual achievement.
